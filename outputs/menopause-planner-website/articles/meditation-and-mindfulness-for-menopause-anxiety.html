Finding Calm: 5 Mindfulness Tips to Ease Menopause Anxiety

Researched and fact-checked by The Menopause Planner Editorial Team. We are not licensed medical practitioners — consult a qualified healthcare provider for personal advice. See our Medical Disclaimer.

Feeling overwhelmed by anxiety during menopause? You're not alone. Let's explore simple, actionable mindfulness techniques that can help you find a sense of calm and control during this transition.

Start Small: 5 Minutes to Center Yourself

When anxiety hits, the idea of a long meditation session can feel daunting. That's why starting with just 5 minutes of focused mindfulness can be incredibly effective.

During perimenopause and menopause, fluctuating hormones can wreak havoc on our emotions, making anxiety more frequent and intense. This can lead to feeling overwhelmed and unable to cope.

Even a brief mindfulness practice can help regulate your nervous system and bring you back to the present moment. It's about consistent practice, not perfect performance. Start with what feels manageable and gradually increase the duration as you become more comfortable.

Try This

TRY THIS: Set a timer for 5 minutes. Sit comfortably, close your eyes, and focus on your breath. Notice the sensation of the air entering and leaving your body. When your mind wanders, gently guide your attention back to your breath.

Breathe, Don't Battle: Focus on Breath Awareness

A common misconception is that meditation requires emptying your mind completely. Instead, mindfulness is about observing your thoughts without judgment, especially your breath.

The hormonal shifts of menopause can trigger a cascade of thoughts and worries, often making it hard to quiet your mind. Fighting these thoughts only amplifies them.

Instead of trying to clear your thoughts, gently guide your attention back to your breath each time you notice your mind wandering. Feel the rise and fall of your chest or abdomen. This simple act anchors you in the present moment and creates a space between you and your worries.

Try This

TRY THIS: Place one hand on your chest and the other on your abdomen. As you breathe in, feel your abdomen rise. As you breathe out, feel it fall. Focus on this physical sensation to ground yourself.

Nature's Embrace: Grounding with Sound

Immersing yourself in nature sounds is a powerful way to ground yourself and calm your nervous system. It's especially helpful when feeling anxious or overwhelmed.

Estrogen decline can make us more sensitive to stress and external stimuli. Nature sounds offer a gentle and soothing alternative to the chaos of daily life.

Research suggests that exposure to nature sounds can lower cortisol levels (the stress hormone) and promote relaxation. You can find nature soundscapes online or simply sit near an open window and listen to the sounds around you.

Try This

TRY THIS: Find a recording of nature sounds (rain, ocean waves, birdsong) and listen to it for 10 minutes. Close your eyes and imagine yourself in that natural setting.

Savor the Moment: Mindful Eating

Mindful eating involves paying full attention to the experience of eating – the colors, textures, smells, and tastes – without distractions. It’s about nourishing your body and your mind.

During menopause, changes in metabolism and appetite can lead to emotional eating or feeling disconnected from your body's needs. This can exacerbate anxiety and create a cycle of unhealthy habits.

By eliminating distractions like phones or TV and focusing on each bite, you can reconnect with your hunger cues, savor your food, and reduce feelings of anxiety associated with eating. It can also help you feel more satisfied with smaller portions.

Try This

TRY THIS: Choose one meal today to eat mindfully. Turn off all screens, sit at a table, and focus on the sensations of eating. Chew slowly and deliberately, noticing the flavors and textures.

Shift Your Focus: From Worry to Awareness

Anxiety often stems from dwelling on the past or worrying about the future. Mindfulness encourages you to shift your focus to the present moment, where you have the most control.

The uncertainty and changes that come with menopause can fuel anxious thoughts about what's to come. Learning to stay present can help you manage these worries.

When you catch yourself worrying, gently acknowledge the thought without judgment, and then redirect your attention to your senses – what you see, hear, smell, taste, and touch. This simple practice can break the cycle of anxiety and bring you back to the present.

Try This

TRY THIS: Throughout the day, pause and ask yourself: 'What am I experiencing right now?' Notice what you're seeing, hearing, feeling, and smelling. Simply observe without judgment.

FAQ

Can meditation really help with menopause anxiety?

Yes, many women find that regular meditation and mindfulness practices can significantly reduce anxiety symptoms. By focusing on the present moment, you can lessen the grip of worries and find a sense of calm.
