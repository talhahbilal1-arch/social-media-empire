Pelvic Floor Exercises That Actually Prevent Leaks During Menopause

The first time I sneezed and peed a little, I was standing in the cereal aisle at Target. I was 49, thought I was handling perimenopause pretty well, and suddenly realized my body had other plans. I stood there mortified, then texted my sister who replied: "Welcome to the club. Get yourself some good underwear and start doing your Kegels."

She wasn't wrong about either thing. But here's what I learned: there's so much more to pelvic floor health during menopause than just squeezing and hoping for the best. And ignoring it doesn't make it better—it makes it worse.

Let me share what actually worked for me and what the research says about keeping your pelvic floor strong during this transition.

Why Your Pelvic Floor Weakens During Menopause

When estrogen drops during perimenopause and menopause, it affects every connective tissue in your body—including the muscles and ligaments that support your bladder, uterus, and bowel. These pelvic floor muscles literally hold everything up, and without estrogen, they lose elasticity and strength.

Add in years of childbirth, chronic coughing, heavy lifting, or even chronic constipation, and you've got a perfect storm for bladder leaks, pelvic pressure, or even pelvic organ prolapse.

I'm not trying to scare you. I'm telling you this because understanding why this happens helps you take it seriously. This isn't about getting old or giving up. This is about a specific hormonal change that requires a specific response.
