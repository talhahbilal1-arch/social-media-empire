## Why Are My Allergies Suddenly Worse?

Think your allergies are *just* allergies this year? Perimenopause might be the puppet master behind the scenes. Let's uncover how hormonal changes can turn spring blooms into your worst nightmare.

It's a sneaky connection, but the hormonal rollercoaster of perimenopause can mess with your immune system, potentially making you more susceptible to allergies. According to the American Academy of Allergy, Asthma & Immunology (2023), hormonal changes during perimenopause can indeed affect the immune system, potentially making women more susceptible to allergies.

It’s not just *getting* allergies that might be new for you, but the *severity* of them as well. And, according to the North American Menopause Society (2022), women in perimenopause are 2-4 times more likely to report new or worsening allergy symptoms compared to their premenopausal years. That's a *huge* jump!

Many women think that allergies are just a childhood thing and that if they didn't have them before, they won't get them later in life. I totally get that thinking! But, as I've learned, hormonal shifts can trigger new sensitivities we never had before. It's like our bodies are suddenly overreacting to things that never bothered us before.

## The Perimenopause-Allergy Connection: What's Really Going On?

The culprit? Estrogen, or rather, the *fluctuating* levels of it.

A 2017 study showed that estrogen can modulate the activity of mast cells, which are key players in allergic reactions (Gomez, E., et al., 2017). Mast cells are like tiny alarm systems in your body that release histamine, the chemical that causes all those annoying allergy symptoms like itching, sneezing, and congestion. When estrogen levels fluctuate, mast cells may become more reactive, leading to increased allergy symptoms. In short, your immune system is more likely to overreact to things like pollen and pet dander.

It’s all connected too. I’ve found that when my allergies are bad, my sleep is worse, which exacerbates my hot flashes, which makes me more stressed… you see the vicious cycle? That's why it’s so important to tackle this from multiple angles. It's not just about popping an antihistamine (though those can help, too!), but about supporting our bodies holistically.

## Natural Estrogen Boosters That May Ease Allergy Symptoms

Okay, so what can we do? We can't control the pollen count (unfortunately!), but we *can* influence our hormone balance naturally.

Now, when I say "estrogen boosters," I'm not talking about hormone replacement therapy (HRT) *necessarily* here (though that's a valid option to discuss with your doctor!). I'm talking about natural ways to support your body's estrogen levels and modulate that immune response, which might, in turn, ease allergy symptoms. It's about giving our bodies the tools they need to cope with the hormonal shifts and the allergic onslaught.

One avenue that research suggests we can take is incorporating phytoestrogens into our diets. Research indicates that phytoestrogens (plant-based estrogens) may have anti-inflammatory properties that could help alleviate allergy symptoms by modulating the immune response, according to the National Institutes of Health (2021). Think of them as little helpers that can gently nudge your estrogen levels in the right direction.

Here are a few natural estrogen boosters that I’ve explored:

*   **Flaxseeds:** These little seeds are packed with lignans, which are phytoestrogens. I sprinkle them on my oatmeal, add them to smoothies, or even bake them into muffins. Just make sure they're ground, so your body can actually absorb the nutrients. I buy them already ground, because let's be honest, who has time to grind flaxseeds every day?
*   **Soy:** Soybeans, tofu, tempeh, and edamame are all great sources of isoflavones, another type of phytoestrogen. I’m partial to a good tofu stir-fry with lots of veggies. I try to buy organic soy whenever possible, just to be on the safe side.
*   **Sesame Seeds:** Another great option to add to your salads or snacks. I love toasting them lightly for a nutty flavor.
*   **Chickpeas:** Hummus anyone? I love snacking on hummus with veggies, so this is a win-win for me. It's such an easy and delicious way to get some extra phytoestrogens into my diet.

## Foods Rich in Phytoestrogens: Your Allergy-Fighting Grocery List

Building on the last section, here's a deeper dive into the grocery list:

*   **Nuts and Seeds:** Almonds, walnuts, sunflower seeds, and pumpkin seeds are all good sources of healthy fats and phytoestrogens. I like to keep a bag of mixed nuts on hand for a quick and easy snack.
*   **Fruits:** Apples, berries, and peaches contain smaller amounts of phytoestrogens, but every little bit helps! Plus, they're packed with antioxidants, which are great for overall health.
*   **Vegetables:** Cruciferous vegetables like broccoli, cauliflower, and Brussels sprouts are beneficial for overall hormone balance. I try to roast a big batch of these veggies at the beginning of the week so I have them on hand for meals.

Now, a note of caution: Phytoestrogens aren't a magic bullet. They might not work for everyone, and it's important to incorporate them as part of a balanced diet, not as the *only* solution. And, of course, talk to your doctor before making any major dietary changes, especially if you have any underlying health conditions.

## Lifestyle Changes to Tame Allergy Flare-Ups

Beyond diet, certain lifestyle changes can make a big difference in managing allergy symptoms during perimenopause. Remember, it’s about supporting your body’s natural defenses! It's about creating a holistic approach to managing our health during this transitional time.

*   **Manage Stress:** Stress can wreak havoc on your hormones and immune system, making allergies worse. Find healthy ways to manage stress, like yoga, meditation, or spending time in nature. Even just 15 minutes of quiet time each day can make a difference. I love to sit outside with a cup of tea and just breathe – it does wonders!
*   **Prioritize Sleep:** Easier said than done, I know, especially with perimenopause! But getting enough sleep is crucial for immune function. Aim for 7-8 hours of quality sleep each night. I’ve found that a consistent bedtime routine helps immensely. I try to go to bed and wake up around the same time every day, even on weekends. [Related: Natural Sleep Aids for Menopause](/articles/natural-sleep-aids-menopause/)
*   **Stay Hydrated:** Drinking plenty of water helps to thin mucus and keep your nasal passages clear. I carry a water bottle with me everywhere and try to refill it throughout the day.
*   **Use a Neti Pot:** Rinsing your nasal passages with a saline solution can help to remove allergens and relieve congestion. It might feel a little weird at first, but it really does work! I do it every morning during allergy season.
*   **Air Purifier:** A good air purifier in your bedroom can help remove allergens from the air while you sleep. I wish I'd gotten one of these sooner!
*   **Clean Regularly:** Dust, vacuum, and wash your bedding frequently to minimize allergens in your home. I try to wash my bedding at least once a week.
*   **Limit Exposure:** On high pollen days, try to stay indoors with the windows closed. If you must go outside, wear a mask and sunglasses. I know it's not the most fashionable look, but it can really help!

## When to Talk to Your Doctor About Hormone Therapy

While natural remedies can be helpful, there are times when hormone therapy might be a necessary consideration. Dr. Elizabeth Boham, MD, MS, RD, an Integrative Medicine Physician, advocates for a holistic approach, including lifestyle modifications like stress reduction and adequate sleep. She also notes that bioidentical hormone therapy may be considered in some cases, *in consultation with a physician*.

If your allergy symptoms are severe and significantly impacting your quality of life, talk to your doctor about your options. Hormone therapy, in some cases, may help to stabilize estrogen levels and reduce allergy symptoms. It's a personal decision, and it's important to weigh the risks and benefits with your doctor's guidance.

Also, rule out other potential causes. What you think are allergies could be a sinus infection, a cold, or something else entirely. I know I've misdiagnosed myself more than once! It's always best to get a professional opinion.
