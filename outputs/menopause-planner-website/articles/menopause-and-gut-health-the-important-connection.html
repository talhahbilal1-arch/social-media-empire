Menopause Gut Health: The Connection You Need to Know (2024)

Researched and fact-checked by The Menopause Planner Editorial Team. We are not licensed medical practitioners — consult a qualified healthcare provider for personal advice. See our Medical Disclaimer.

Feeling bloated, gassy, or just generally off since menopause started? You're not alone. Gut health and hormonal changes are deeply connected. Here's how to nourish your gut for a more comfortable menopause journey.

The Menopause-Gut Connection

Menopause brings big changes, and often, that includes shifts in your gut. You might notice more bloating, gas, or changes in bowel habits. These symptoms can be uncomfortable and frustrating during an already challenging time.

Estrogen plays a role in maintaining the integrity of your gut lining and influencing the balance of your gut bacteria. As estrogen levels decline during menopause, this balance can be disrupted, leading to gut issues.

Supporting your gut health can ease these symptoms and improve your overall well-being during menopause. Focus on nourishing your gut with the right foods and habits.

Try This

TRY THIS: Start a food journal. Track what you eat and how you feel afterward. This helps identify trigger foods that are upsetting your gut.

Feed the Good Bugs: Prebiotic Power

Prebiotics are the food source for the beneficial bacteria in your gut. Eating prebiotic-rich foods helps these good bacteria thrive, which in turn supports better digestion and overall gut health.

Think of prebiotics as fertilizer for your gut garden. They help the good bacteria grow and flourish, outcompeting the less desirable strains. This leads to a more balanced and resilient gut microbiome.

Excellent prebiotic sources include garlic, onions, leeks, asparagus, bananas, and oats. Incorporate these into your daily meals to nurture your gut.

Consider supplementing with acacia fiber if you are not getting enough prebiotics from food sources.

Try This

TRY THIS: Add a sliced banana to your morning oatmeal or yogurt for a prebiotic boost to start your day.

Cut Back on Gut Busters: Processed Foods

Processed foods, high in sugar and unhealthy fats, can wreak havoc on your gut. They often contain additives and preservatives that disrupt the balance of your gut flora and promote inflammation.

These foods can feed the 'bad' bacteria in your gut, leading to an imbalance that contributes to bloating, gas, and other digestive issues. Plus, they often lack the nutrients your gut needs to thrive.

Focus on whole, unprocessed foods like fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains. These provide the nutrients and fiber your gut needs to stay healthy.

Read labels carefully and try to avoid foods with long lists of artificial ingredients.

Try This

TRY THIS: Swap one processed snack each day for a handful of nuts or a piece of fruit.

Stress and Your Gut: A Two-Way Street

Stress can have a significant impact on your gut health. The gut and brain are closely connected through the gut-brain axis, and stress can disrupt this communication, leading to digestive problems.

When you're stressed, your body releases hormones like cortisol, which can affect gut motility and inflammation. This can manifest as constipation, diarrhea, or general discomfort.

Practice stress-management techniques like yoga, meditation, or deep breathing exercises. These can help calm your nervous system and support a healthier gut.

Even a few minutes of mindfulness each day can make a difference.

Try This

TRY THIS: Take a 5-minute break to practice deep breathing. Inhale slowly for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, and exhale slowly for 4 counts. Repeat several times.

Consider Probiotics: Adding Good Bacteria

Probiotics are live microorganisms that can benefit your gut health. They help replenish and diversify your gut flora, promoting better digestion and overall well-being.

During menopause, a probiotic supplement may help restore balance to your gut after shifts due to hormonal fluctuations.

Look for a probiotic supplement with a variety of strains and a high CFU (colony-forming units) count. You can also get probiotics from fermented foods like yogurt, kefir, and sauerkraut.

It's best to speak with your doctor or a registered dietitian before starting any new supplement.

Try This

TRY THIS: Add a serving of plain yogurt with live and active cultures to your daily routine.

Hydration Station: Water for Wellness

Staying hydrated is crucial for optimal digestion and gut health. Water helps move food through your digestive tract and prevents constipation.

Dehydration can slow down digestion and lead to a buildup of waste in your colon, contributing to discomfort and bloating.

Aim to drink at least eight glasses of water per day. You can also hydrate with herbal teas, fruits, and vegetables with high water content.

Carry a water bottle with you and sip throughout the day.

Try This

TRY THIS: Set a reminder on your phone to drink water every hour. Keep a water bottle visible as a cue.

FAQ

Can menopause cause new food sensitivities?

Yes, hormonal changes during menopause can affect gut permeability, potentially leading to new or heightened sensitivities. It's important to identify triggers and eat accordingly.

How long does it take to improve gut health during menopause?

Improving gut health is a gradual process, and results vary. However, many women notice positive changes within a few weeks of making dietary and lifestyle adjustments.
