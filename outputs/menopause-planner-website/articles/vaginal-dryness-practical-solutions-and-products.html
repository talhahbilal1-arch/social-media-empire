5 Vaginal Dryness Fixes That Actually Work (Plus the Products That Helped Me)

Let's talk about something that affects about 50% of us during menopause but rarely comes up at dinner parties: vaginal dryness. I'm going to be straight with you—this was the symptom that blindsided me. I'd braced myself for hot flashes and mood swings, but nobody mentioned that sex would suddenly feel like sandpaper, or that I'd feel uncomfortable just sitting in certain positions.

Here's what I've learned through my own experience and countless conversations with other women: vaginal dryness isn't something you just have to live with. There are actual solutions that work. Some are simple over-the-counter fixes, others require a doctor's visit, but all of them can make a real difference.

Why This Happens (The Quick Version)

Before we get to solutions, let's understand what's going on. As estrogen levels drop during perimenopause and menopause, the tissues in our vaginal area become thinner, less elastic, and produce less natural moisture. This isn't about being "turned on enough"—it's a physical change in tissue health.

The medical term is vaginal atrophy or genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM), and it doesn't just affect sex. It can cause discomfort during exercise, make you more prone to UTIs, and just generally make you feel off. You're not imagining it, and you're definitely not alone.

Fix #1: Start With a Daily Vaginal Moisturizer (Not Just Lube)

This was a game-changer for me, and I wish someone had explained the difference sooner. A vaginal moisturizer is different from a lubricant—you use it regularly (every 2-3 days) whether you're having sex or not. It actually helps restore moisture to the tissue, not just provide temporary slickness.

I use mine at bedtime, just like I use face moisturizer. Within about two weeks, I noticed less everyday discomfort. The research backs this up—studies show that regular use of vaginal moisturizers can significantly improve tissue health and reduce discomfort.

Look for products that are specifically designed for vaginal use (not regular body lotion!). They should be pH-balanced and free from irritating ingredients like glycerin, parabens, and fragrances.

Fix #2: Get Serious About Lubricants (The Right Ones)

This is the fix that honestly saved my sex life. But here's what I learned the hard way: not all lubricants are created equal.

Silicone-based lubricants last longer and don't get sticky, which was a revelation for me. Water-based ones work too, but you'll need to reapply more often. What you want to avoid are lubes with warming sensations, flavors, or "tingling" effects—they often contain ingredients that can irritate already sensitive tissue.

Apply it generously. I mean really generously. More than you think you need. And here's a tip that helped me: apply it to your partner too, not just yourself. It makes everything smoother and takes the pressure off.

Fix #3: Consider Vaginal Estrogen (Talk to Your Doctor)

This is where you absolutely need to have a conversation with your healthcare provider, but I want you to know this option exists because it was transformative for me.

Local vaginal estrogen (creams, tablets, or rings inserted directly into the vagina) is different from systemic hormone therapy. It uses very low doses that stay mostly in the vaginal area, with minimal absorption into your bloodstream. For many women, it's considered safe even when oral hormone therapy isn't recommended.

After about three months of using vaginal estrogen, my tissue health improved dramatically. The dryness lessened, sex became comfortable again, and I stopped getting frequent UTIs. The research is clear that it's one of the most effective treatments for vaginal atrophy.

Your doctor needs to be part of this decision, especially if you have a history of hormone-sensitive cancers or other contraindications. But please don't rule it out without at least asking.

Fix #4: Try Supplements That Support Hormone Balance Naturally

While supplements won't directly reverse vaginal atrophy the way estrogen can, some women find they help with overall menopause symptoms relief, which can indirectly improve tissue health and libido.

Evening Primrose Oil contains gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), which some women find helpful for hormone balance naturally during menopause transitions.

Maca Root is traditionally used to support energy and libido, and while the research is mixed, some women swear by it.

Vitamin D3 isn't specifically for vaginal dryness, but maintaining adequate vitamin D levels supports overall health during menopause, including bone and immune health.

Be patient with supplements—they take at least 6-8 weeks to show any effects, and they don't work for everyone. Always talk to your doctor before starting, especially if you're on other medications.

Fix #5: Keep Things Active Down There

I know this sounds ironic when sex is uncomfortable, but regular sexual activity (with a partner or solo) actually helps maintain vaginal health. Blood flow to the area keeps tissues healthier and more elastic.

If penetration is off the table right now, that's completely okay. Other types of sexual activity still increase blood flow to the area. And yes, masturbation counts—and might actually be easier while you're figuring out the lubrication situation.

Using your menopause planner to track which solutions you're trying and how they're working can help you figure out what combination works best for you.

The Bottom Line

Vaginal dryness during perimenopause and menopause is incredibly common, but that doesn't mean you should just accept being uncomfortable. Start with the over-the-counter solutions—moisturizers and good lubricants can make a huge difference. If those aren't enough, talk to your doctor about vaginal estrogen. It's not "giving up" to use medical treatment; it's taking care of yourself.

What worked best for me was a combination: daily vaginal moisturizer, silicone lube when needed, and eventually vaginal estrogen. It took some trial and error, and about three months before everything felt "normal" again.

You deserve to feel comfortable in your body and to have a satisfying sex life if that's what you want. This symptom doesn't have to be permanent, and you don't have to figure it out alone. Start with one thing from this list this week, and give yourself permission to keep adjusting until you find what works.
