Why Most Supplement Lists Are Complete Garbage

Google "best menopause supplements" and you'll find lists that recommend 15-20 supplements. Here's why they're unreliable: they're written by supplement companies, influencers paid by companies, or wellness bloggers with zero scientific training. They cherry-pick studies that show positive results and never mention the studies that found nothing.

There's a financial incentive to recommend everything. Sell more supplements, make more commission. The cost to you: confusion, wasted money, and a supplement cabinet that looks like a pharmacy.

This guide uses only peer-reviewed research from independent medical journals. I focus on effect size (will you actually notice a difference?) and study quality (how solid is the evidence?). I'll tell you what works, what might work, and what to skip entirely.

The Tier System: How to Evaluate Evidence

Strong evidence: Multiple large, well-designed studies across different populations all found the same positive effect. The effect size is clinically meaningful—you'll notice it in 2-4 weeks. These supplements are worth trying.

Moderate evidence: Some studies show benefit, but not all. Or the effect is small (10-15% improvement). These might help, but they're not a guarantee. Try them after you've optimized the strong-evidence tier.

Weak evidence: One or two small studies found something, but it's not reproducible or the effect is tiny. Skip these unless you have specific symptoms they might address.

Weak Evidence Tier (Skip Unless Specific Symptoms)

Red clover isoflavones: One large study found no effect. Marketing hype is strong. Skip it.

Sage: Two studies showed modest hot flash reduction (10-15%), but both were small and funded by supplement companies. Not enough evidence yet.

Evening primrose oil: Research is mixed. Some studies show slight benefit for hot flashes, others show nothing. If you have specific joint pain, it might help, but magnesium and omega-3 are better bets first.

Chasteberry (Vitex): Good evidence for perimenopause-era hormone balance, not for postmenopausal symptoms. If you're still cycling, worth trying. If you're fully postmenopausal, skip it.

What NOT to Buy (Even Though Companies Market Them)

"Menopause support" proprietary blends: You don't know the dose of each ingredient. A blend might contain 5 mg of black cohosh and 1 mg of red clover—too low to be effective. Buy individual supplements so you control the dose.

Supplements marketed with before-and-after testimonials: If the only evidence is customer reviews, it's not evidence. Skip it.

Anything claiming to "replace HRT": Nothing replaces HRT. Supplements help with some symptoms; HRT addresses the root cause (missing estrogen). Be honest about what you're looking for.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are most menopause supplement recommendations garbage?

Most supplement lists are written by companies selling supplements or influencers paid by companies. They cherry-pick studies, exaggerate results, and never mention the studies that found no effect. There's no incentive to be honest about what doesn't work. This guide uses only peer-reviewed research from independent medical journals.

What's the difference between strong and moderate evidence?

Strong evidence means multiple large, well-designed studies across different populations all found the same positive effect, and the effect size is clinically meaningful (you'll actually notice it). Moderate evidence means some studies show benefit but not all, or the effect is small. Weak evidence means one or two small studies found something, but it's not reproducible.

Can you just take every supplement that has any evidence?

No. Supplements have doses, interactions with medications, and side effects. Taking 10 supplements creates complexity and uncertainty. Prioritize the strong-evidence tier first, assess their impact over 4 weeks, then add moderate-evidence supplements if needed. Most women see good results from a simple protocol: magnesium, vitamin D, and omega-3.

The Bottom Line

Menopause supplements work, but only if you choose based on evidence, not marketing. Start with magnesium and vitamin D—both have strong research backing. Add black cohosh and fish oil if specific symptoms persist. Skip the proprietary blends and testimonial-driven products.

Supplements aren't a replacement for HRT if you need it, but they're a solid first step for sleep, mood, and inflammation. Give each supplement 4 weeks before deciding if it works. And remember: the best supplement is consistency. Inconsistent dosing undermines everything.
