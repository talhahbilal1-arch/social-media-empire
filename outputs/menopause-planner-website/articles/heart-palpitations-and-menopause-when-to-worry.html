Heart Palpitations and Menopause: 5 Signs You Need to Call Your Doctor

The first time my heart suddenly started racing while I was sitting at my desk, I thought I was having a heart attack. My chest felt tight, my pulse was pounding in my ears, and I couldn't figure out what was happening. Turns out, it was just another delightful perimenopause symptom nobody warned me about.

Heart palpitations are incredibly common during perimenopause and menopause—some studies suggest up to 40% of us experience them. The fluctuating estrogen levels affect our cardiovascular system in ways that can feel genuinely scary. But here's what you need to know: while most palpitations are benign and hormone-related, some signs mean it's time to get checked out right away.

I'm not a doctor, and you should absolutely talk to yours about any heart symptoms. But as someone who's been through this and done extensive research, I want to help you understand when palpitations are just annoying and when they need immediate attention.

Racing Heart with Chest Pain or Dizziness

This is the big one. If your heart is racing AND you're experiencing chest pain, pressure, or significant dizziness, call your doctor immediately or head to the ER. Don't mess around with this combination.

Normal menopause-related palpitations might make your heart race, but they shouldn't come with chest pain that feels like pressure, squeezing, or tightness. When I had my hormone-related palpitations, my heart would flutter or pound, but there was no pain—just that uncomfortable awareness of my heartbeat.

The difference matters. Chest pain with palpitations could indicate a cardiac event, especially if you have risk factors like high blood pressure, diabetes, or a family history of heart disease. Women often experience heart attacks differently than men, with symptoms that can include unusual fatigue, nausea, or back pain alongside chest discomfort.

Dizziness that makes you feel like you might pass out is another red flag. Light-headedness from standing up too quickly? That's common in menopause and usually related to blood pressure changes. But dizziness that accompanies rapid heartbeat and makes you need to sit or lie down immediately needs evaluation.

Palpitations Lasting More Than a Few Minutes

Hormone-related palpitations typically come and go quickly. Mine would last anywhere from a few seconds to maybe a minute—uncomfortable, yes, but brief. If your heart is racing or beating irregularly for more than a few minutes, especially if it's happening frequently, that warrants a conversation with your doctor.

Extended palpitations could indicate an arrhythmia or other heart condition that needs monitoring. Your doctor might want to do an EKG or have you wear a Holter monitor for 24-48 hours to catch what's happening when you feel these episodes.

Here's what helped me track my patterns: I started using a simple notebook to record when palpitations happened, how long they lasted, what I was doing, and what I'd eaten or drunk. This turned out to be invaluable when I finally saw my doctor because I could show her clear patterns. (Spoiler: caffeine and sugar were major triggers for me during perimenopause, even though they never bothered me before.)

The tracking also helped me distinguish between palpitations that lasted 30 seconds (annoying but manageable) and ones that went on longer (which I brought to my doctor's attention). Most of mine were triggered by hot flashes or stress, and they'd settle down as quickly as they started.

Shortness of Breath That Doesn't Ease Quickly

Palpitations paired with shortness of breath can be tricky because anxiety—which is rampant during menopause—can cause both symptoms. But if you're experiencing genuine difficulty breathing that doesn't improve within a minute or two, don't ignore it.

I remember one episode where my heart was pounding and I felt like I couldn't catch my breath. I sat down, tried some deep breathing, and within about 90 seconds, everything calmed down. That's typical for anxiety-related symptoms. But if you're struggling to breathe, if your lips or fingernails look bluish, or if the breathlessness is severe, get emergency help.

The connection between estrogen and our respiratory system is real. Estrogen receptors exist in our lungs and airways, so hormonal fluctuations can affect breathing. Some women develop exercise-induced asthma during menopause or notice their existing asthma gets worse. This is separate from cardiac issues but still needs medical attention.

Irregular Heartbeat with Fainting or Near-Fainting

An irregular heartbeat—where your heart skips beats, adds extra beats, or feels like it's flip-flopping—is common in menopause. I had these "skipped beat" sensations regularly, especially at night when I was lying down. My doctor called them PVCs (premature ventricular contractions) and assured me they were benign after testing ruled out anything serious.

But if you actually faint or come close to fainting when you have an irregular heartbeat, that's a different story. Syncope (fainting) related to heart rhythm issues needs immediate evaluation because it could indicate a serious arrhythmia.

Near-fainting is subjective, I know. For me, the difference was clear: feeling light-headed and knowing I should sit down versus that gray-out sensation where you're about to lose consciousness. If you're experiencing the latter with heart irregularities, please get checked out.

Your doctor might order an echocardiogram, stress test, or refer you to a cardiologist. This isn't about being dramatic—it's about ruling out conditions like atrial fibrillation or other arrhythmias that are more common as we age and that menopause can sometimes unmask.

New Palpitations Different from Your Usual Pattern

Once you've been dealing with menopause symptoms relief for a while, you get to know your body's quirks. I knew my heart would flutter during hot flashes. I knew lying on my left side sometimes triggered awareness of my heartbeat. These became my "normal."

What you need to watch for is a NEW pattern that feels different. Maybe you've had occasional skipped beats, but suddenly they're happening in clusters. Or you've never had palpitations before and now they're showing up regularly. Or the sensation changes—from fluttering to pounding, or from occasional to daily.

Any significant change in your pattern deserves a call to your doctor. Our bodies change during menopause, yes, but new symptoms can also indicate new conditions that have nothing to do with hormones. As we age, our risk for various heart conditions increases, and it's better to check things out than assume everything is menopause-related.

I also found that Vitamin D3 made a difference in my overall cardiovascular health—many of us are deficient, and vitamin D plays a role in heart health and mood regulation.

When Palpitations Are "Just" Menopause

After all these serious warnings, let me reassure you: most heart palpitations during menopause ARE hormone-related and benign. Once my doctor ruled out cardiac issues, I learned to manage mine through lifestyle changes.

What helped me:

  • Cutting way back on caffeine (painful but effective)
  • Managing stress through yoga and meditation
  • Staying hydrated
  • Avoiding large meals close to bedtime
  • Limiting alcohol, which I discovered was a huge trigger
  • Getting regular exercise, which actually strengthened my cardiovascular system
  • Addressing menopause weight gain, which reduces strain on your heart

The drop in estrogen affects our autonomic nervous system, which controls heart rate. It also affects our blood vessels' ability to dilate and constrict properly. These changes can absolutely cause palpitations without anything being "wrong" with your heart.

Moving Forward with Confidence

Getting your heart checked out isn't overreacting—it's smart self-care. I saw my primary care doctor first, who did an EKG and basic blood work to check my thyroid (thyroid issues can also cause palpitations and often emerge during perimenopause). When everything came back normal, we could confidently attribute my symptoms to hormonal changes.

Keep your menopause planner updated with symptom tracking. Note what triggers your palpitations, how long they last, and any accompanying symptoms. This information helps your doctor tremendously and helps you feel more in control.

Remember: chest pain with palpitations, extended episodes, severe shortness of breath, fainting, or new patterns all warrant medical attention. Everything else can probably wait for a regular appointment, but don't hesitate to call your doctor's office if you're worried. That's what they're there for.

You're not imagining these symptoms, and you're not overreacting. Your body is going through massive changes, and sometimes those changes feel scary. But with the right information and medical support, you can navigate this with confidence.

Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, get it checked. And if it turns out to be "just menopause"? Well, at least you'll know for sure, and you can focus on finding what brings you relief.
