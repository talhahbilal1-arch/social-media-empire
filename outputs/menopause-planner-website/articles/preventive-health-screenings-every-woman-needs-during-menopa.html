5 Essential Health Screenings That Protect Your Health During Menopause

I learned this the hard way: that nagging fatigue I blamed on menopause? Turned out my thyroid had quietly given up the ghost. My vitamin D was in the basement. And my bone density scan showed osteopenia—early bone loss I had no idea was happening.

Here's what nobody tells you about menopause: it's not just about hot flashes and mood swings. Our bodies are going through massive changes that show up in blood work, bone scans, and other tests before they become serious problems. But you have to know what to ask for.

Your regular annual exam probably isn't catching everything that matters right now. After going through this myself and talking to countless women in the same boat, I've put together the five screenings that actually make a difference during this transition. These aren't optional nice-to-haves—they're the tests that catch heart disease, bone loss, and hormonal chaos before they derail your life.

Bone Density Scan: Your Protection Against Fractures

The first time my doctor suggested a bone density scan, I thought she was being overly cautious. I was only 51, active, and felt strong. But here's the reality: we lose up to 20% of our bone density in the first five years after menopause because estrogen protects our bones, and now it's gone.

A DEXA scan takes about 15 minutes and doesn't hurt. It measures the density of your hip and spine bones—the ones most likely to fracture as we age. The results give you a T-score that tells you if you have normal bone density, osteopenia (early bone loss), or osteoporosis.

Get your baseline scan at the start of menopause, then follow up every two years. If your results show bone loss, there's so much you can do: weight-bearing exercise, calcium and vitamin D supplementation, and sometimes medication. But you can't fix what you don't know about.

Talk to your doctor about scheduling this scan, especially if you have risk factors like smoking, a thin frame, or a family history of osteoporosis. Mine showed osteopenia, which pushed me to start strength training and get serious about my Vitamin D3—I take this specific brand because it's high-quality and actually absorbs well.

Lipid Panel: The Heart Disease Wake-Up Call

This one saved my life. Not dramatically with sirens and emergency rooms, but quietly, with numbers on a page that told me my cholesterol had jumped 40 points in two years.

Before menopause, estrogen helps keep our cholesterol in check and our arteries flexible. After menopause, heart disease becomes our number-one killer—more than breast cancer, more than anything else. Yet most of us are still focused on mammograms (important!) while ignoring our cardiovascular risk.

A complete lipid panel measures your total cholesterol, LDL (the "bad" kind), HDL (the "good" kind), and triglycerides. You want this test done annually during menopause because things can change fast. Don't just accept "your cholesterol is fine" from your doctor—ask for the actual numbers and what they mean for your heart disease risk.

When my LDL crept into concerning territory, I made changes: more fiber, daily walks, cutting back on wine. Six months later, my numbers had improved without medication. But I never would have known to make those changes without seeing those test results.

Research shows that focusing on hormone balance naturally through diet, exercise, and stress management can positively impact your cholesterol levels, but you need that baseline to know where you stand.

Thyroid Function Test: Not Just Menopause Fatigue

The exhaustion, brain fog, weight gain, and hair thinning? I blamed menopause symptoms relief medications and perimenopause tips for months. Turns out my thyroid was barely functioning.

Here's the tricky part: thyroid problems and menopause symptoms overlap so much that doctors often miss thyroid issues. About 20% of women develop thyroid problems during menopause, usually hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid). The symptoms—fatigue, weight gain, feeling cold, constipation, depression—look exactly like what we expect from hormone changes.

Insist on a complete thyroid panel, not just TSH. You want TSH, Free T3, Free T4, and thyroid antibodies. I've heard from too many women whose TSH was "normal" but their T3 was low, and they felt awful until they found a doctor who looked at the complete picture.

Once I started thyroid medication, my energy returned within weeks. The weight that wouldn't budge finally started responding to my efforts. The brain fog lifted. Don't assume everything is menopause—get your thyroid checked annually.

Vitamin D Levels: The Bone Loss Connection

My vitamin D was 22 ng/mL when it should be between 40-60. My doctor said at least 70% of her menopausal patients are deficient.

Vitamin D isn't just about bone health, though that's crucial during menopause when bone loss accelerates. Low vitamin D is linked to worse hot flash remedies natural effectiveness, menopause weight gain, mood problems, and increased inflammation.

You need a simple blood test called 25-hydroxyvitamin D. Get this checked at least once a year, maybe twice if you're supplementing and trying to get your levels up.

Most of us need to supplement—you can't get enough from food or sunshine, especially if you live anywhere that has winter. When my levels were low, my doctor started me on 5,000 IU daily. I use magnesium glycinate alongside my vitamin D because magnesium helps your body actually use the vitamin D, and it also helps with sleep and muscle cramps.

Within three months of supplementing, my level hit 52 ng/mL, and I genuinely felt the difference in my energy and mood. Using your menopause planner to track symptoms alongside vitamin D supplementation can help you see the connection too.

Hormone Panel: Before Symptoms Take Over

This is the controversial one, but hear me out. Not every woman needs extensive hormone testing, but if you're struggling with severe symptoms—crushing fatigue, debilitating hot flashes, complete loss of libido, or depression—a comprehensive hormone panel gives you data to work with.

I'm talking about estradiol, progesterone, testosterone, DHEA-S, and cortisol. These paint a picture of where your hormones actually are, not where your doctor assumes they should be based on your age.

Some doctors dismiss hormone testing, saying "of course your hormones are low, you're menopausal." But the specifics matter, especially if you're considering hormone replacement therapy or trying to support hormone balance naturally through supplements.

When my testosterone came back almost undetectable, everything clicked—the vanished libido, the muscle loss despite strength training, the complete lack of motivation. My doctor and I used those numbers to create a treatment plan that actually addressed what my body needed.

If you're exploring natural remedies like maca root, having baseline hormone levels helps you track whether these supplements actually work for your body—I found maca helped with my energy and mood, though it didn't dramatically shift my hormone numbers.

Talk to your doctor about whether hormone testing makes sense for your situation. It's not necessary for everyone, but if you're really struggling, it provides valuable information.

Your Next Steps

Print this article and take it to your next doctor's appointment. Write down which of these tests you've had recently and which you need to request. Don't wait for your doctor to suggest them—be proactive about your health.

Schedule your bone density scan if you haven't had one yet. Request a complete lipid panel and thyroid function test at your next annual exam. Ask about checking your vitamin D levels.

These tests aren't about finding problems—they're about catching issues early when you can actually do something about them. They're about taking control of your health during a time when your body is changing in ways you can't always feel.

I wish someone had given me this checklist five years ago. I would have caught my thyroid issue sooner, started protecting my bones earlier, and saved myself months of feeling terrible while thinking it was just menopause.

You deserve to feel good during this transition. These five screenings help make that possible.
