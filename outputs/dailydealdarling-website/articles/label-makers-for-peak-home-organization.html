5 Label Maker Secrets That Actually Work (#3 Saved Me)

I bought my first label maker thinking I'd become one of those people with a perfectly organized pantry. Instead, I labeled everything like a maniac and somehow made my kitchen more confusing. Turns out, there's a right way and a very wrong way to use these things.

After two years of trial and error (and peeling off way too many labels), I've figured out what actually works. These aren't the obvious tips you'll find on Pinterest. These are the real-deal shortcuts that made me stop opening every single container to find what I need.

Label the Top, Not the Front—Seriously

This one sounds so simple, but it completely changed how I use my pantry and fridge. Think about it: when you're looking into a cabinet or drawer, you're looking down at your containers, not straight ahead. Those pretty front-facing labels? Useless unless everything is lined up in a single perfect row.

I learned this the hard way after spending an afternoon labeling all my storage organizer bins with beautiful labels on the front. Then I stacked them. Couldn't see a single label. Had to pull each one out to find what I needed.

Now I label the tops, and I can literally grab what I need in the dark. It works for spice jars, snack containers, craft supplies, whatever. The only exception? Open shelving where everything's at eye level. Then yeah, go ahead and label the fronts.

Pro tip: if you're using bins with lids, put the label on the lid itself. That way you're not stuck with a labeled container when you want to repurpose it later.

Put Expiration Dates on Everything You Open

This tip alone has probably saved me hundreds of dollars in wasted food. I used to open a bag of flour, a jar of tahini, or a bottle of fish sauce and have absolutely no idea when I opened it or when it would go bad.

Now? I slap a label with the date opened on basically everything that doesn't have an obvious expiration date. Opened snacks, condiments, baking supplies, even vitamins. Game-changer for the fridge especially—I'm looking at you, mystery sauce that's been there for three months.

My label format is simple: "Opened: [date]" or if I know the shelf life, I'll write the actual expiration date. For example, most opened nuts go bad in 3 months, so I'll calculate that and write "Use by: [date]."

This works amazing with meal prep containers too—label the date you prepped your food and you'll actually know if that chicken is still safe to eat or if it's science experiment territory.

The best part? No more sniff tests. No more "does this smell weird to you?" conversations with my husband. Just check the label and make a decision like a rational human.

Clear Bins Plus Labels Beat Fancy Baskets Every Single Time

Listen, I love a good woven basket as much as the next person. They look gorgeous on Instagram. But you know what's better than reaching into a cute basket and digging around blindly? Actually seeing what you have.

Clear bins with labels are not as aesthetically pleasing, but they are so much more functional. I can see exactly how much coffee I have left, spot when I'm running low on something, and—this is the big one—I actually use what I buy instead of forgetting it exists.

I replaced all my "pretty" pantry baskets with clear bins last year and it's probably the best organizing decision I've made. No more buying duplicate items because I couldn't see I already had one buried in the back of a basket.

The label just seals the deal. Clear bin showing pasta + label on top = I can find it in two seconds even when I'm rushing to make dinner and everything feels chaotic.

If you really can't give up the aesthetic, here's my compromise: use clear bins inside cabinets where function matters, and save the pretty baskets for stuff you don't access daily, like extra linens or seasonal items.

Label Both Ends of Your Charger Cords (Life-Changing)

Okay, this one isn't technically home organization ideas in the traditional sense, but it has saved my sanity more times than I can count.

We have approximately 847 white charging cords in our house. Phone chargers, iPad chargers, laptop chargers, random battery pack chargers. They all live in a drawer and they all look identical.

I started putting tiny labels on BOTH ends of each cord—one label at the USB end and one at the plug end. So I can grab the right cord without playing "guess which device this charges" every morning when I'm trying to get out the door.

Label both ends because you usually can't see both ends at once when cords are tangled together. Trust me on this.

This also works great for kitchen gadgets worth buying like air fryer accessories or random appliances that share similar-looking cords. Label them once, never confuse them again.

The key is using a label maker for this instead of tape or those twist ties. The labels stay put through normal use and actually look clean, not like you went crazy with a label gun (even though you absolutely did).

Skip Labeling What's Obviously Visible

Here's where I went wrong initially: I labeled EVERYTHING. Flour in a clear container? Labeled. White sugar in a clear container? Labeled. A mason jar full of obviously visible chocolate chips? You bet I labeled it.

Waste of time. Waste of labels. Waste of energy.

If I can clearly see what's in a container, it doesn't need a label. Save your labels for stuff that actually needs identification—like different types of flour (all-purpose vs. bread vs. whole wheat), different sugars (white vs. caster vs. powdered), or things in opaque containers.

The exception is if you're going for a specific aesthetic and you want everything uniform. Fine, label away. But from a pure functionality standpoint? Label what's confusing, not what's obvious.

This also means you can actually maintain your system. Over-labeling made me feel like updating anything was a huge project. Now I only label what matters, and keeping things organized feels way less overwhelming.

Making It Actually Work for You

The real secret to label makers isn't buying the fanciest one or creating the most beautiful labels. It's about using them strategically in ways that actually make your life easier, not just Instagram-worthy.

Start with one problem area—maybe it's your pantry, your junk drawer, or that cabinet full of mystery cords. Apply whichever of these tips makes sense for that space. See if it actually helps. Then expand from there.

I keep my label maker in my kitchen drawer now instead of stored away with craft supplies. The easier it is to grab, the more likely I am to actually use it when I need it. Which means my systems stay organized instead of falling apart three weeks later.

These home organization ideas aren't about perfection. They're about making your daily routine a little bit easier, a little less frustrating. And honestly? That's the best kind of organizing there is.
