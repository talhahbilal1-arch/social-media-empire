The 5 Hosting Essentials I Actually Use All Summer (No. 3 Changed Everything)

I used to think hosting meant having matching serving platters and those fancy tiered stands that cost a fortune. Then I hosted my first backyard gathering with a mismatched collection of stuff, and honestly? Nobody cared about my plates. They cared about the food, the vibe, and not dying of heat exhaustion on my patio.

After three summers of regular hosting (and some embarrassing fails), I've figured out what actually matters. These five things get pulled out constantly, and I'm not talking about stuff that looks pretty but sits unused. I mean the things that make hosting easier and more enjoyable for everyone, including me.

A Decent Cooler That Doesn't Leak All Over Your Deck

This sounds basic, but hear me out. I suffered through two summers with a cheap cooler that somehow always had a puddle underneath it. The ice melted in three hours, drinks were lukewarm by dinner, and I spent half the party running inside for fresh ice.

A good cooler keeps drinks cold for the entire evening, which means you're not constantly managing beverage temperatures like it's your second job. Look for one with thick insulation and a drain plug that actually seals. I got mine on sale last year, and the difference is ridiculous. Ice lasts 12+ hours, no puddles, and people can help themselves without me playing bartender all night.

The real bonus? You can prep it the night before. Throw everything in with ice, and you're done. That's one less thing to stress about when people are arriving and you're still trying to figure out why your grill won't light.

The Right Serving Situation (Hint: It's Not What Instagram Told You)

Those beautiful wooden boards everyone uses for charcuterie? They're gorgeous. They're also heavy, hard to clean, and kind of a pain to store. I own one, and I pull it out maybe twice a year for photos.

What I actually use: large, flat serving platters or even sheet pans. You can fit way more food, they're easy to carry, and cleanup takes thirty seconds. I have three matching white platters from Target that cost like fifteen bucks each, and they've hosted everything from burgers to fruit to an entire taco spread.

For the charcuterie situation specifically (because people do love a good cheese board), I use parchment paper directly on my counter or table. Sounds weird, looks amazing, requires zero cleanup. Just toss it when you're done. This is one of those best home finds that actually simplifies your life instead of complicating it.

The key is having enough serving pieces that you're not playing Tetris trying to fit everything. But they don't need to match or cost a fortune. Function over fancy is my hosting motto now.

An Air Fryer for When You Need to Cook A Lot Without Heating Up Your Kitchen

Okay, this one genuinely changed my hosting game. Summer means it's already ninety degrees outside, and turning on your oven makes your kitchen feel like an actual sauna. Enter: the air fryer.

I was skeptical for so long because I thought it was just another kitchen gadget taking up space. Wrong. This thing cranks out appetizers, reheats leftovers perfectly, and cooks side dishes without making your AC work overtime. I've made everything from crispy potatoes to wings to roasted vegetables, all while my kitchen stays bearable.

The Cosori Air Fryer is one of those kitchen gadgets worth buying if you host regularly—it's big enough to actually feed people, not just make sad single servings.

Last weekend, I cooked frozen appetizers for twelve people using just the air fryer and grill. My kitchen stayed cool, I didn't have to time everything perfectly with one oven, and cleanup was a breeze. If you host more than twice a summer, you'll use this constantly.

Bulk Ice Cube Situation (This Sounds Boring But Trust Me)

Nobody talks about ice until you run out at 7 PM with a houseful of people. Then it becomes a whole thing where someone has to make an ice run, and you're stuck rationing cubes like they're gold.

I bought two extra ice cube trays, and now I start making ice three days before any gathering. I fill gallon bags and stuff them in the freezer. Sounds excessive? Maybe. But you know what's better than running out of ice? Not running out of ice.

You can also freeze water bottles—they keep the cooler cold AND give people ice-cold water as they thaw. This is such a simple home organization idea, but it eliminates so much last-minute stress. Plus, pre-frozen water bottles mean you're not buying bags of ice that cost six bucks at the gas station.

String Lights and a Bluetooth Speaker That Actually Work

I lumped these together because they solve the same problem: creating an atmosphere that makes people want to stay longer. Good lighting and decent music transform your backyard from "sitting in a yard" to "actually fun place to hang out."

String lights don't need to be expensive to look good. I bought mine for twenty bucks, hung them once, and they've been up for two summers. They make everything look prettier and more intentional, even if you forgot to clean your patio furniture.

For speakers, you don't need the fanciest model. You need one that's loud enough to actually hear over conversation, holds a charge, and doesn't cut out every five minutes. I've had the same portable speaker for three years, and it still works perfectly. The key is keeping it charged (learn from my mistakes and charge it the night before).

This combo turns your space into somewhere people genuinely enjoy being. It's one of those affordable home decor upgrades that makes a huge difference for minimal money. Plus, good vibes are the best gift ideas for women hosting this summer—aka yourself.

What Actually Matters When You're Hosting

After hosting a bunch, I've realized the perfect party has nothing to do with having everything match or look Pinterest-worthy. It's about making it easy for people (including you) to relax and have fun.

These five things solve real problems: keeping drinks cold without constant maintenance, serving food efficiently, cooking without overheating your kitchen, having enough ice, and creating a comfortable atmosphere. That's it. Everything else is extra.

Start with what actually stresses you out about hosting, and solve those problems first. For me, it was temperature control (both food and environment) and not wanting to spend the entire party in the kitchen. Your pain points might be different, and that's fine. Focus on what makes hosting easier for you specifically.

The best part? Most of this stuff isn't expensive. We're talking basic equipment and a little planning. Save your money for the food and drinks people will actually remember.

Now go host something. Your backyard is calling.
