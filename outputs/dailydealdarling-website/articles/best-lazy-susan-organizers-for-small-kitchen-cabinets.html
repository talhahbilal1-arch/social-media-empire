Why Lazy Susans Are Perfect for Small Kitchen Cabinets

When you're working with limited square footage, you can't afford to waste a single shelf. A good lazy susan lets you store twice as much in the same amount of space because everything becomes visible and reachable with just a simple spin. The key for small cabinets is choosing the right size. Too big and it won't spin properly or fit at all. Too small and you're not maximizing your space. That's where measuring comes in handy—which we'll talk about in a minute.

Choosing the Right Size Lazy Susan for Your Space

Before you buy anything, grab a measuring tape. Seriously, this five-minute step will save you from the disappointment of an organizer that doesn't fit.

For small cabinets, you're typically looking at lazy susans between 6 and 12 inches in diameter. Here's how to figure out what you need:

Measure your cabinet width and depth. For corner cabinets, measure from the corner to the cabinet door—that's your maximum diameter. Subtract about an inch to ensure smooth rotation without hitting the sides.

Consider your cabinet height too. Standard lazy susans are great, but tiered versions give you even more storage in the vertical space. If you've got decent height to work with (12+ inches), definitely go for a two-tier option.

Think about what you're storing. Tall bottles need deeper edges (at least 2-3 inches) so things don't fly off when you spin. Spice jars can work with shallow trays. Canned goods do well with non-slip surfaces.

For most small kitchen cabinets, a 9-inch or 10-inch turntable hits the sweet spot. It's large enough to be useful but compact enough to fit in tight spaces. I have two 10-inch lazy susans in my cabinets and they're absolutely perfect for standard cabinet sizes.

Best Lazy Susan Styles for Small Spaces

Not all lazy susans are created equal, and different cabinet situations call for different solutions. Let me break down the main types you'll see:

Single-tier turntables are the classic option—a simple rotating tray that works beautifully in shallow cabinets or for pantry shelves. These are perfect for spice organization, condiment bottles, or baking supplies. They're usually the most affordable option too, with plenty of choices under $15.

Two-tier lazy susans double your storage capacity vertically. I love these for corner cabinets where height isn't an issue. You can store everyday items on the top tier and less-used things on the bottom. A two-tier turntable organizer typically runs between $20-30 and is worth every penny for the extra storage.

Corner cabinet lazy susans are specifically designed with a wedge shape or kidney shape to maximize those weird corner spaces. Some even come as pull-out systems, though those tend to be pricier and more complicated to install.

Divided lazy susans have sections that keep different items separated as they spin. These are fantastic if you're organizing multiple categories—like keeping your oils separate from your vinegars, or your baking supplies sorted by type.

For small cabinets specifically, I recommend starting with a quality lazy susan turntable for small cabinets in the 9-10 inch range. You can always add more once you see how much easier your life becomes!

Materials Matter: Plastic vs. Acrylic vs. Bamboo

Here's where personal preference comes in, but each material has its pros and cons for kitchen use.

Plastic lazy susans are budget-friendly, lightweight, and easy to clean. Most run under $15, and they're totally functional. The downside? They can look a bit cheap and may not hold up as well over time with heavier items.

Acrylic or clear turntables are my personal favorite for upper cabinets because they look sleek and let you see everything clearly. They're still affordable (usually $15-25) and feel more substantial than basic plastic. The transparency also makes it easier to spot what you need at a glance.

Bamboo or wood options are gorgeous and sturdy—great for heavier items like canned goods or appliances. They add a warm, natural look to your cabinets. The trade-off is they cost a bit more ($20-35) and can stain if you spill liquids, so they're better for dry goods storage.

For small kitchen cabinets, I'd go with acrylic or good-quality plastic. They're durable enough for daily use, easy to wipe down when spills happen, and won't break the bank. Save the fancy bamboo for visible spaces like countertop organization.

Installation and Organization Tips

Good news: most lazy susans require zero installation. You literally just place them in your cabinet and start organizing. But here are some tips to make them work even better:

Use drawer liner underneath to prevent slipping. A little bit of non-slip shelf liner keeps the lazy susan from spinning the entire base along with your items.

Group similar items together but don't overcrowd. Leave a little space between items so you can grab what you need without knocking other things over.

Put taller items toward the center and shorter items around the edges for better visibility and balance.

Label if you're using multiple lazy susans in the same cabinet or pantry. A simple label on the edge helps you remember what's on each one without having to spin them all.

Clean them regularly. Pull them out once a month, wipe them down, and toss any expired items. Lazy susans make this so much easier than traditional shelf organization.

I also love using lazy susans in unexpected places beyond kitchen cabinets—under the bathroom sink for toiletries, in the fridge for drinks and jars, even on my craft desk for supplies. Once you start using them, you'll find opportunities everywhere.
