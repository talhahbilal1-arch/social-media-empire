I Tested $100 Worth of Temu Kitchen Gadgets So You Don't Have To

Okay, confession time: I went down a serious Temu rabbit hole at 11 PM one night and woke up to seventeen package notifications. In my defense, everything looked AMAZING in those videos, and when you're spending $2.47 on a garlic press, what's the worst that could happen?

Well, I found out. But I also found some genuinely shocking winners that have completely changed my mind about ultra-cheap kitchen gadgets. I bought the same items from both Temu and Amazon (when I could find them) to see if the price difference actually matters. Spoiler: sometimes it does, sometimes it absolutely doesn't.

The Silicone Spatula Set Drama

I ordered matching sets of silicone spatulas from both places—three-piece sets with different sizes, both around $4. They looked identical in photos. They were NOT identical in real life.

The Temu spatulas arrived first (surprisingly), and honestly looked fine. Nice colors, felt sturdy enough. Then I used one to stir my pasta sauce. At medium-high heat. The edge of the spatula literally started MELTING into my marinara. I'm talking visible warping and this concerning smell that made me dump the entire pot.

The Amazon set? I've been using them for three weeks now, including some serious high-heat scrambled egg action, and they're completely fine. Zero melting, zero weird smells. This is one of those times where the extra $3 matters because, you know, I'd prefer not to eat melted mystery plastic.

If you want spatulas you can actually trust, the HomeHero Silicone Spatula Set has held up beautifully in my kitchen and they're heat-resistant up to 480°F—I've tested this claim thoroughly.

The Garlic Press That Made Me Question Everything

Here's where things get interesting. I bought a stainless steel garlic press from Temu for $3.12 and a nearly identical-looking one from Amazon for $9.99. I was fully prepared for the Temu version to be garbage.

Plot twist: the Temu garlic press is actually BETTER. I'm as shocked as you are.

I tested them side by side with the same cloves of garlic. The Temu press required less force, pushed the garlic through cleaner, and was easier to clean afterward. The Amazon one worked fine, but it felt slightly flimsier and left more garlic chunks un-pressed. I've used the Temu press probably forty times now and it's still going strong.

This is genuinely one of the best home finds I've stumbled across in months, and I feel weird saying that about something from Temu. But I'm not going to lie to you—if it works, it works. Though I will say, this seems to be the exception rather than the rule based on everything else I tested.

The Measuring Cup Situation (AKA The Waiting Game)

Remember when I said the spatulas arrived surprisingly fast from Temu? Yeah, that was a fluke. The measuring cups took FORTY-ONE DAYS to arrive. Forty-one! I had completely forgotten I ordered them.

By day 12, I caved and ordered a set from Amazon that showed up in two days. Which is probably what Temu is banking on—you forget you ordered something cheap, then buy it elsewhere anyway, and they're not actually out anything if you don't bother returning the late item.

The measuring cups themselves were fine when they finally arrived—accurate measurements, decent quality. But here's the thing: when you need measuring cups, you usually need them NOW because you're trying to bake something. Waiting a month and a half for a $2.87 set of measuring cups isn't the bargain it seems like when you end up buying another set anyway.

For kitchen gadgets worth buying that you'll actually receive this decade, Amazon Prime is worth it. The two-day shipping isn't just convenience—it's reliability.

The Vegetable Peeler That Lasted Three Potatoes

This one hurt because the peeler was SO CUTE. It had this ergonomic grip in a pretty sage green color, and it was only $1.84. I was making mashed potatoes for dinner and grabbed my new Temu peeler feeling very pleased with my frugal self.

Potato one: peeled beautifully.

Potato two: still going strong.

Potato three: the blade literally popped out of the handle mid-peel.

I tried snapping it back in. It fell out again immediately. This peeler had ONE JOB and it couldn't even make it through a single meal prep session. The Amazon peeler I bought cost $5.99 and I've peeled approximately 30 pounds of vegetables with it at this point without a single issue.

Sometimes the cheapest option costs you more because you end up replacing it immediately. A good vegetable peeler is one of those unglamorous kitchen gadgets worth buying once and never thinking about again. The Temu version made me think about it A LOT (mostly curse words).

The Knife Sharpener Showdown

For this test, I deliberately dulled one of my knives (it hurt my soul, but it was for science). Then I split the blade into sections and sharpened half with the Temu sharpener ($2.41) and half with the Amazon version ($11.99).

The Temu sharpener made a great grinding sound and definitely did... something. The knife was sharper than before, but in a weird uneven way. Some parts cut tomatoes beautifully, other parts just squished them.

The Amazon sharpener created an actually uniformly sharp edge that's held up for weeks. I've sharpened three different knives with it now and they all cut like they're supposed to. If you're serious about cooking (or even just medium-serious), this is another case where spending the extra money makes a real difference.

That said, if you need a quality knife to sharpen in the first place, the Knife Set by Home Hero has been my go-to recommendation for anyone who wants professional-quality blades without the professional price tag.

So What's Actually Worth Buying?

After burning through $100 and several weeks of testing, here's what I learned: Temu can be great for things that don't involve heat, don't require precision, and aren't critical to your actual cooking process. That garlic press? Genuinely good. Decorative stuff? Usually fine. Things that touch hot food or need to be sharp? Hard pass.

Amazon's slightly higher prices usually mean better quality control, much faster shipping, and way easier returns if something goes wrong. For kitchen essentials and self care products you use daily, it's worth the extra few dollars.

The real bargain isn't always the lowest price—it's buying something once that actually works instead of replacing it three times. Trust me, I learned this the expensive way so you don't have to.

If you're stocking your kitchen from scratch, focus on quality for the basics first. A coffee maker that works every morning is worth more than five cheap ones that break, and the same goes for most kitchen gadgets worth buying.

Save your Temu shopping for the stuff that's genuinely low-stakes—fun organizer bins for your junk drawer, cute ice cube trays, that weird avocado slicer you don't really need but kind of want. For everything else? Don't gamble on your dinner.
