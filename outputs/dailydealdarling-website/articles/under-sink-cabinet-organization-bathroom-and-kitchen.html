Under Sink Cabinet Organization That Actually Works (No More Avalanche When You Open It)

You know that moment when you open your under-sink cabinet and something falls out? Or you're digging around behind the pipes trying to find that one cleaning spray you KNOW is in there somewhere? Yeah, I was living that chaos for way too long.

I finally got fed up last month when I knocked over an entire bottle of dish soap while searching for sponges. The thing is, under-sink spaces are genuinely weird to organize. You've got pipes in the way, awkward depths, and somehow these cabinets become black holes where products go to die.

But I've actually figured this out, and my bathroom and kitchen cabinets are now spaces I don't dread opening. Here's what legitimately works.

Stop Trying to Stack Things (Seriously, Just Stop)

This was my biggest mistake for years. I'd buy stuff, shove it under the sink, and try to stack bottles and containers like Tetris. Spoiler: it never worked. Everything would topple over, and I'd spend five minutes excavating just to grab dish soap.

The solution isn't stacking—it's creating levels. I picked up a simple expandable shelf organizer, and honestly, it changed everything. You place it around the pipes (most adjust), and suddenly you've got two usable surfaces instead of one chaotic pit.

What I love about this approach is you can actually SEE what you have. No more buying duplicate cleaning products because you forgot you already had glass cleaner. In my kitchen, I keep daily-use items on the bottom level (dish soap, sponges, dishwasher pods) and backstock or less-used stuff on the upper shelf.

For the bathroom, same concept. Bottom level gets my hair dryer and styling tools, top shelf holds extra toiletries and backup toilet paper. Game changer.

Use the Cabinet Door (That's Free Real Estate)

I don't know why it took me so long to realize the inside of the cabinet door is perfectly good storage space just sitting there unused. Face palm moment when I figured this out.

I installed simple adhesive hooks on the inside of my kitchen cabinet door, and now my dish gloves and small scrub brushes have an actual home. No more throwing them in a heap at the bottom of the cabinet. In the bathroom, I use these hooks for hair ties, headbands, and my round brush.

You can also get those over-the-door organizers with pockets. I was skeptical about these initially because I thought they'd look messy, but the clear-pocket ones actually help you see everything at a glance. I use mine in the bathroom for travel-size toiletries, makeup remover wipes, and face masks—all the little stuff that used to float around loose and get lost.

Contain the Chaos With the Right Bins

Here's where people usually go wrong with under-sink organization—they buy cute bins that don't actually fit the space or serve the purpose. I learned this the hard way after buying three different sets before finding what worked.

The trick is getting bins that are tall and narrow rather than short and wide. Under-sink cabinets have vertical space you're probably wasting. Those squat, wide bins might look nice, but they don't maximize the awkward cabinet dimensions.

I switched to organizer bins that are taller and can slide in and out easily, and it's so much better for corralling all those random bottles and spray cleaners that used to tip over constantly.

For the bathroom, I keep one bin for hair stuff (extra shampoo, conditioner, hair spray), another for skincare backstock, and a third for cleaning supplies. In the kitchen, I have separate bins for dishwasher supplies, cleaning sprays, and trash bags. Being able to pull out the entire bin instead of reaching around pipes is such a small thing that makes a huge difference.

Just make sure whatever you get has handles or is easy to grip. I had some bins without handles at first, and trying to pull them out was annoying enough that I stopped maintaining the system.

Deal With the Pipes (They're Not Going Anywhere)

Those pipes under your sink are the biggest pain in organizing this space, but you've gotta work with them, not against them.

For narrow pipes, a tension rod is genuinely brilliant. Install it across the cabinet opening, and you can hang spray bottles by their triggers. This keeps them upright, easy to grab, and they're not taking up any floor space in the cabinet. I do this in both my kitchen and bathroom, and it's one of those solutions that feels almost too simple to work but absolutely does.

If you have a bulkier pipe situation (looking at you, garbage disposal), you need organizers specifically designed to fit around plumbing. There are U-shaped shelf units and pull-out drawers with cutouts. I haven't personally tried the pull-out drawer system because my cabinets are rentals and I'm not drilling into them, but friends who've installed them swear by them.

The other thing with pipes—don't be afraid to use the space NEXT to them. I have a skinny little trash can that fits perfectly beside my kitchen pipes and holds extra trash bags. In the bathroom, I keep a narrow container next to the pipes for toilet bowl cleaner and the plunger (yes, it lives under the sink now, not awkwardly beside the toilet).

Create Zones (Even in This Small Space)

This sounds overly organized, but hear me out. When you assign specific purposes to specific areas of your under-sink cabinet, you stop just tossing things in randomly.

In my kitchen, the left side is for dishes and dishwasher stuff. The right side is for surface cleaning (countertop spray, glass cleaner, all-purpose cleaner). The back is for less-used items like oven cleaner and descaling solution. This way, I'm not pawing through everything every time I need something.

Same in the bathroom. Left side is daily hair stuff and styling tools. Right side is cleaning supplies and extra toilet paper. Back area is backup toiletries and first aid supplies. These best home finds aren't expensive, and they're simple tweaks that eliminate so much daily frustration.

The beauty of this zoning system is it also helps you notice when you're running low on something BEFORE you run out completely. I can glance under my kitchen sink and see my dish soap is getting low, so I add it to my shopping list instead of discovering I'm out when I need it.

Actually Use This Space Better

Look, I'm not going to pretend that organizing under your sink is life-changing in some dramatic way. But removing this one small annoyance from your daily routine legitimately makes life a bit easier. No more avalanches when you open the door. No more buying duplicates because you can't find what you already own. No more wasting time digging around for basic supplies.

Start with one cabinet. Don't try to tackle both bathroom and kitchen in one afternoon unless you're way more motivated than I am. I did my kitchen first, saw how much better it worked, and that motivated me to tackle the bathroom the following weekend.

The organizers you need don't have to be expensive. I've mixed cheap dollar store bins with slightly nicer expandable shelves, and it all works together fine. What matters is creating a system that makes sense for what YOU actually store under there.

And if you're still dealing with chaos in other parts of your home, these same principles apply. Levels, zones, and containers that actually fit your space—that's the formula for home organization ideas that stick instead of falling apart after two weeks.
