5 Presidents Day Furniture Tricks That Expose Fake Sales (2026)

Okay, real talk: I almost spent $1,200 on a mattress last Presidents Day thinking I was getting an incredible deal. Then I checked the price history and wanted to throw my phone across the room. The "50% off" sale price was literally what the mattress cost in December.

I've spent the last three years tracking furniture and mattress prices around major sales, and Presidents Day is the absolute worst for fake discounts. But here's the thing—there ARE legitimate deals hiding in there. You just need to know what you're looking at.

Let me show you exactly how to separate the real savings from the marketing BS.

Check January 1st Baseline Prices First

This is my non-negotiable first step, and it takes maybe five minutes. On January 1st, furniture retailers set their "starting" prices for the year. Then they slowly inflate them through January and early February so their Presidents Day "discounts" look massive.

I track about 15 retailers every year, and the pattern is so consistent it's almost funny. That couch listed at $2,499 with "40% off" during the sale? Go look at what it cost on New Year's Day. I guarantee it was probably $1,799 or less.

Here's what I do: I take screenshots of anything I'm considering on January 1st. Just open your notes app, screenshot the product page with the price visible, and date it. If you missed January 1st this year, check archive sites like Wayback Machine—they sometimes capture retail pages.

Last year, I caught West Elm doing this with a dining table. January 1st price: $1,299. February 10th (pre-sale): $2,199. Presidents Day "sale" price: $1,499. They wanted me to feel grateful for paying $200 MORE than the January price.

This Retailer Raised Prices 35% Before 'Discounts'

I'm going to name names because this genuinely made me angry: Mattress Firm raised prices an average of 35% across their most popular models between January 15th and February 10th last year. Then they slapped "Presidents Day Savings" banners everywhere.

Their Serta Perfect Sleeper model went from $799 (early January) to $1,299 (February 9th) to a "sale price" of $899. That's not a sale. That's a markup with extra steps.

But here's who actually keeps their pricing honest: Costco and IKEA. I know IKEA isn't glamorous, but their prices stay remarkably consistent year-round. If they say something is on sale, it's actually cheaper than usual, not just cheaper than last week's inflated price.

Target is also pretty reliable for smaller furniture pieces and organizer bins—these are perfect for finally getting your bedroom furniture drawers under control and they're genuinely affordable without waiting for fake sales.

The Mattress Brands with Genuinely Low Prices

After three years of this research rabbit hole, I've found exactly three mattress brands that don't play pricing games: Casper, Tuft & Needle, and Costco's Novaform line.

These brands keep their prices stable all year, so when they DO have a sale, it's real. Casper rarely discounts more than 15%, but that 15% is legitimate. I bought my Tuft & Needle during a random Tuesday in March for the same price they advertised on Presidents Day—which actually made me trust them more.

The luxury mattress brands (I'm looking at you, Tempur-Pedic and Beautyrest) are the absolute worst offenders. Their "sales" are often just bringing the price down to what they should cost anyway. A $4,000 mattress isn't a better deal at $2,400 when a comparable Costco mattress is $899 every single day.

If you're going to buy a traditional mattress from a furniture store, at least do it right: wait until February 18th or 19th (more on that in a minute), bring up the price history on your phone, and negotiate. I've literally shown sales associates my January screenshots and gotten an extra 10% off because they knew I wasn't playing.

Screenshot Trick That Catches Fake Markups

This trick has saved me literally thousands of dollars. Download a browser extension called Keepa or CamelCamelCamel. Yes, these are primarily for Amazon price tracking, but the concept works everywhere.

For non-Amazon retailers, I use a simple system: I screenshot products I'm watching on the 1st and 15th of every month. I keep them in a folder on my phone organized by store name. Takes maybe ten minutes total.

But here's the power move: before you buy ANYTHING over $300 during Presidents Day sales, Google the product name plus "price history." There are Reddit threads, deal forums, and shopping blogs tracking this stuff. Someone has probably already done the research.

I found out last year that the "best home finds" I was eyeing at Pottery Barn—this gorgeous media console—had been the exact same "sale" price during Veterans Day, Christmas, and New Year's. It wasn't a Presidents Day deal. It was just... the price.

For kitchen gadgets worth buying, Amazon's price history is actually pretty honest—you can see when a knife set is genuinely discounted versus when they're playing games with the list price.

Best Buying Day Isn't Monday (It's This)

Everyone thinks Presidents Day Monday is when you get the best price. Wrong. The best prices are actually Thursday or Friday of Presidents Day weekend.

Here's why: retailers front-load their sales to capture early shoppers, then they panic when they don't hit their numbers. By Thursday/Friday, they're adding extra discounts, offering better financing, or throwing in free delivery to hit their weekend goals.

I've tested this three years running. Last year, I tracked a sectional sofa at Ashley Furniture. Monday (the holiday): $1,899. Friday before: $1,799 with free delivery and an extra accent chair included. That's $300+ in additional value just for waiting.

The exception: if you're shopping online at retailers with limited inventory, you might need to buy earlier. But for big box furniture stores with warehouses full of stock? Thursday and Friday are your sweet spot.

Also, and I can't stress this enough: don't buy mattresses or furniture from pop-up "sale event" tents in parking lots. I know the signs look tempting, but those are usually overstock items with questionable return policies. Stick with established retailers where you can actually return something if it's terrible.

For affordable home decor that won't break the bank regardless of sales, I always keep my kitchen stocked with reliable basics that don't require waiting for "deals" that aren't actually deals.

What I'm Actually Buying This Year

After all this research, here's what's on my actual shopping list for Presidents Day 2026:

I'm getting a new mattress, but I'm buying it from Costco or waiting for a genuine Casper discount code (they email them pretty regularly if you're on their list). Not falling for department store markup tricks again.

For furniture, I'm checking IKEA first, then Target for smaller pieces. If I find something I love at a traditional furniture store, I'm screenshotting it, checking the price history, and either buying it Thursday/Friday of Presidents Day weekend or walking away.

The truth is, most furniture and mattresses don't vary that much in quality within the same price tier. A $1,500 couch is a $1,500 couch whether you bought it in February or June. The only difference is whether you got tricked into paying $1,800 for it because you thought you were saving money.

Your best strategy? Know your prices, trust consistent retailers, and remember that the best deal is the one where you pay the actual fair price—not an inflated price with a fake discount sticker slapped on top.

Save your screenshots, do your homework, and maybe I'll see you in the Costco mattress aisle on February 19th.
