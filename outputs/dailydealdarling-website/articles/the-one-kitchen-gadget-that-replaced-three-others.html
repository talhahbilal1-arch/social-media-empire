What I Actually Use It For (The Honest List)

Rotisserie chicken every Sunday: This is the function that made me get rid of my countertop rotisserie. Two-pound chicken takes about 45 minutes, comes out with crispy skin and stays juicy inside. I use the leftovers for lunches all week—chicken salad, tacos, grain bowls. Saves me probably $20/week compared to buying pre-made rotisserie chickens.

Dehydrating fruit and jerky: My old dehydrator had four trays and took 12 hours to dry apple slices. This one has two racks but better air circulation—apples are done in 6 hours. I make turkey jerky for my husband's hiking trips. The kids think the banana chips are candy.

Reheating leftovers without the microwave sadness: Pizza, french fries, fried rice—all the things that get weird and soggy in the microwave come back to life in here. Three minutes at 350°F and leftover pad thai tastes fresh.

Actual baking: I've made muffins, small batch cookies, and even a 7-inch cake in here. Preheats in 3 minutes versus 10-15 for my full oven. In summer when I don't want to heat up the whole kitchen? This is everything.

Roasted vegetables that actually get crispy: Brussels sprouts, broccoli, cauliflower—they all get those caramelized edges I could never achieve consistently in my oven. Twenty minutes and they're done.

The Learning Curve (What I Wish I'd Known)

It's not completely intuitive out of the box. The rotisserie basket is different from the rotisserie spit—took me one ruined chicken to figure that out. The basket is for smaller items like wings or vegetables that you want to tumble. The spit is for whole chickens or roasts.

The dehydrate function runs at a lower temperature than air frying, so you can't just press a button and walk away without checking the manual first. I burned my first batch of kale chips by using the air fry setting instead.

You need to hand wash the rotisserie accessories. The basket and drip tray are dishwasher safe, but the spit and forks are not. Not a dealbreaker, but worth knowing.

Counter space: measure before you buy. I had to rearrange my coffee station to make room, and it needs about 5 inches of clearance on all sides for proper ventilation. If your kitchen is truly tiny, this might not be the solution.
