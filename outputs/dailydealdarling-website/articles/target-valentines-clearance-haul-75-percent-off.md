## What to Skip: Valentine's Clearance That's Not Worth It

Even at 75% off, some stuff is junk. Psychologist Dr. April Lane Benson, who specializes in shopping behavior, says the key difference between smart clearance shopping and compulsive buying is intentionality. Before you buy something, ask yourself: "Will I actually use this?" and "Where will I store it?"

**Hard pass on:**

- **Cheap, waxy chocolate** – Discount pricing doesn't make bad chocolate taste good. If you wouldn't eat it at full price, don't buy it on clearance.
- **Single-use decor** – A banner that says "Be My Valentine" will sit in a bin for 11 months. That's not savings; that's clutter.
- **Perishables you won't eat quickly** – Check expiration dates. If that candy expires in three weeks and you're not sharing it, you're just wasting money. According to the USDA, food waste accounts for 30-40% of the food supply, and seasonal candy contributes when people overbuy.

## How to Store Seasonal Clearance Without Becoming a Hoarder

Real talk: if you can't store it properly, don't buy it. I learned this the hard way after finding melted chocolate in my garage in July.

**My storage system:**

- **Candy and chocolate** – Clear plastic bins in a cool, dark closet (not the garage, not the attic). Label with purchase date and expiration date.
- **Gift bags and wrap** – Hanging organizer on the back of my closet door. Takes up zero floor space.
- **Home decor and kitchen items** – If I can't immediately put it into rotation in my house, I don't buy it. The exception: next year's Valentine's gifts for my husband, which go in my designated gift box.
- **Gift box** – One large under-bed storage bin where I keep all clearance finds for future gifts. If the box is full, I stop buying.

This is how you actually save money—getting the stuff you'd buy anyway, just for way less, without your house turning into a warehouse.
