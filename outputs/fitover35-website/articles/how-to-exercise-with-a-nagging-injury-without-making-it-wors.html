How to Exercise With a Nagging Injury: Does it Actually Work for Men Over 35?

I've had a cranky right shoulder for two years now. Started with some stupid bench press form, turned into a constant reminder that I'm not 25 anymore. The pain isn't debilitating — it's that annoying 4/10 that flares up on certain movements and makes me wonder if I'm making it worse every time I train.

Here's what I learned: you can absolutely keep training with nagging injuries. I haven't missed more than three consecutive gym days in those two years, still added 15 pounds of muscle, and my shoulder is actually getting better. But you need a system, not just "push through it" or "take it easy."

The 48-Hour Rule That Changed Everything

First thing I figured out: pain during exercise is different from pain after exercise.

If something hurts while you're doing it — that sharp, "this is definitely wrong" sensation — stop immediately. No debate. But if you get mild discomfort that disappears within 48 hours? You're probably fine to keep training that movement.

My shoulder hurts during overhead press. Like, genuinely hurts. So I don't do overhead press anymore. Simple. But it's fine with lateral raises, even though there's a tiny twinge. That twinge is gone by the next morning. That's my green light.

Track this stuff. I use a basic notes app and rate pain 1-10 after workouts. If it's consistently above 5 or lasting beyond two days, that movement is out. Below 3 and gone by morning? That's just tissue adapting.

Swap Movements, Don't Skip Workouts

When my shoulder acts up, I don't take rest days. I modify. This is the difference between maintaining your muscle building after 35 and losing three months of progress because you "needed to heal."

Here's my actual substitution list for shoulder problems:

  • Barbell bench press → dumbbell floor press (shorter range of motion)
  • Overhead press → landmine press (angled path is shoulder-friendly)
  • Pull-ups → neutral-grip pull-ups or lat pulldowns (less external rotation)

For lower back issues (had those too):

  • Conventional deadlifts → trap bar deadlifts or Romanian deadlifts
  • Back squats → goblet squats or Bulgarian split squats
  • Seated cable rows → chest-supported rows

The pattern: find movements that train the same muscles without hitting your pain point. I actually got better at targeting my lats once I stopped forcing pull-ups and switched to neutral grip. Sometimes constraints make you better.

The 80% Intensity Protocol

This is backed by actual research, not just my experience: training at 80% of your pain-free maximum lets you maintain strength without aggravating injuries.

I can bench press 225 pounds, but at that weight my shoulder complains. So I do sets of 185. That's roughly 82% of my max. I can do 5-6 reps comfortably, get a solid pump, and walk out feeling worked but not damaged.

The numbers: in a 2019 study from the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, lifters who reduced training loads to 80% during minor injuries maintained 93% of their strength over 8 weeks. Those who tried pushing through at 100% either got worse or had to stop training entirely.

Apply this to everything. Running hurts your knee? Do 80% of your normal distance at 80% pace. Even home workout no equipment routines benefit — do push-ups on an incline if flat ones hurt, or go to your knees.

> Pro-Coach Insight: The sweet spot for injury training is 3 sets of 8-12 reps at that 80% load, three times per week for the affected area. This volume keeps muscle protein synthesis elevated without overloading damaged tissue. I rest 90 seconds between sets instead of my usual 60 — gives the joint extra recovery time during the session.

The Progressive Overload Workaround

You can still get stronger with injuries. You just need to be creative about progressive overload.

Can't add weight without pain? Add reps. I stayed at 185 pounds on bench for 8 weeks, but went from 3 sets of 5 reps to 4 sets of 10. That's significant volume increase and my chest still grew.

Can't add reps? Slow down the tempo. 3 seconds down, 1 second up turns easy weight into a serious challenge. My legs responded better to slow goblet squats than they did to heavy back squats anyway.

Can't do either? Add training frequency. Hit the movement twice a week instead of once. More exposure to the stimulus without maxing out any single session.

I maintained my workout plan over 35 through my worst injury phases by cycling through these approaches. Some weeks were volume weeks, some were tempo weeks. All of them kept me progressing.

When to Actually Stop Training

Real talk: sometimes you need to shut it down completely.

If pain increases week-over-week despite modifications, stop. If you're compensating so much that you're creating new problems (my left shoulder started hurting because I was overusing it to protect my right), stop. If simple daily activities hurt, stop.

I took three full weeks off upper body training once. It sucked. But I came back stronger because I actually healed instead of managing chronic inflammation.

Use that time for your weak points. I couldn't train upper body, so I did leg workouts four times a week and added serious core work. My squat went up 30 pounds and I finally got visible abs. Silver lining.
