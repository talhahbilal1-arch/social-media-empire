Push Pull Legs or Upper Lower? Real Talk on Training Splits After 35

Choosing the right training split can make or break your progress, especially after 35. Many guys unknowingly pick a routine that beats up their joints and hinders recovery, leading to frustration and plateaus. Are you making this mistake?

Push Pull Legs (PPL): Frequency vs. Recovery

The Push Pull Legs (PPL) split is structured around grouping exercises by movement pattern: push (chest, shoulders, triceps), pull (back, biceps), and legs (quads, hamstrings, calves). This allows for high-frequency training, hitting each muscle group 2-3 times per week, which can be advantageous for muscle protein synthesis. A 2016 meta-analysis published in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* showed that training frequency can be a significant factor in hypertrophy, provided recovery is adequate.

For men over 35, the increased frequency of PPL can be a double-edged sword. While muscle protein synthesis may be elevated, recovery capacity often diminishes with age. This means that the cumulative stress on joints and the central nervous system can become problematic, leading to overuse injuries and decreased performance.

If you choose PPL, pay close attention to your body's signals. Don't be afraid to deload or take extra rest days when needed. Prioritize proper form and consider lower-impact exercise variations to minimize joint stress.

The Fix

THE FIX: Implement active recovery days between PPL workouts. Light cardio (walking, cycling) for 30-45 minutes, foam rolling, and mobility work can improve blood flow and reduce muscle soreness. Consider supplementing with omega 3 (2-3g daily) to reduce inflammation.

Upper/Lower: Focusing on Intensity and Volume

The Upper/Lower split divides your training into upper body and lower body days. This allows for a higher volume of work per session for each muscle group, with each group typically hit 1-2 times per week. This approach is particularly effective for building strength and muscle mass when combined with progressive overload—gradually increasing the weight, reps, or sets over time.

For men over 35, the Upper/Lower split offers the advantage of more focused recovery time for each muscle group. This can be beneficial for those with joint issues or slower recovery rates. It also allows you to really dial in the intensity on each training day, maximizing muscle fiber recruitment.

With the upper/lower split, you can focus on a rep range of 8-12 for hypertrophy, using compound exercises as the base of the workout. Aim to increase the weight each week by a small amount (2.5-5 lbs) or add an extra rep.

The Fix

THE FIX: Prioritize compound exercises like squats, deadlifts, bench press, and overhead press. These exercises stimulate more muscle growth and strength gains than isolation exercises. Consider supplementing with creatine (5g daily) to improve strength and power output.

Recovery Considerations After 35: The Overlooked Factor

Recovery becomes paramount as we age. Hormonal changes, decreased nutrient absorption, and increased inflammation all contribute to slower recovery times. Ignoring these factors can lead to overtraining, injuries, and decreased performance. A study in *Aging Cell* (2015) showed that muscle protein synthesis rates decline with age, emphasizing the importance of optimizing recovery strategies.

Sleep is non-negotiable. Aim for 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night to optimize hormone production and muscle repair. Nutrition also plays a crucial role. Ensure adequate protein intake (1.6-2.2g per kg of bodyweight) and prioritize whole, unprocessed foods.

Stress management is equally important. Chronic stress elevates cortisol levels, which can impair muscle growth and recovery. Incorporate stress-reducing activities like meditation, yoga, or spending time in nature into your routine.

The Fix

THE FIX: Prioritize sleep hygiene. Create a relaxing bedtime routine, avoid caffeine and alcohol before bed, and ensure your bedroom is dark, quiet, and cool. Consider supplementing with magnesium glycinate (200-400mg) before bed to improve sleep quality.

Joint Health: A Non-Negotiable for Longevity

Joint pain and stiffness are common complaints among older lifters. The repetitive stress of weight training can exacerbate existing joint issues or lead to new ones. Proper form, adequate warm-up, and appropriate exercise selection are crucial for protecting your joints. Avoid exercises that cause pain or discomfort, and don't be afraid to modify movements to accommodate your body's limitations.

Consider incorporating joint-friendly exercises like swimming, cycling, or yoga into your routine. These activities can improve mobility and reduce inflammation. Supplementation with collagen can also be beneficial.

Strength training itself, when done correctly, can improve joint stability and reduce pain. Focus on strengthening the muscles around the joints to provide support and protection.

The Fix

THE FIX: Warm up thoroughly before each workout with dynamic stretching and light cardio. Consider using a foam roller or lacrosse ball to release tight muscles and improve mobility. Supplement with collagen peptides (10-20g daily) to support joint health.

FAQ

Is PPL or Upper/Lower better for building muscle after 35?

Both can be effective. Upper/Lower may be better if you need more recovery time, while PPL can be great if you can recover fast enough to handle the frequency.

How many rest days should I take per week?

Listen to your body. Start with 1-2 rest days and adjust as needed. Overtraining can lead to injury, so don't be afraid to take extra rest when needed.

What rep range is best for building muscle?

Aim for a rep range of 8-12 for most exercises. You can also incorporate some sets in the 6-8 range for strength and some sets in the 12-15 range for hypertrophy.
