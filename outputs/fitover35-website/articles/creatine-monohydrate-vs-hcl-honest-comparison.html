Creatine Monohydrate vs HCL: What Actually Works After 35

I'm going to save you some money right now: creatine HCL costs about 4x more than monohydrate, and I've tested both extensively. Here's what you actually need to know.

When I got serious about fitness at 35, creatine was one of the first supplements I researched. The marketing around HCL made it sound like monohydrate was outdated technology. "Better absorption!" "No bloating!" "Smaller doses!"

I bought into it. Spent $40 on a month's supply of HCL. Then I actually looked at the research and felt pretty stupid.

The Science Nobody Wants You to Know

Creatine monohydrate has over 1,000 studies backing it up. It's the most researched supplement in sports nutrition history. Creatine HCL? Maybe a dozen studies, and none of them show it's significantly better.

Here's what monohydrate does that's proven: increases ATP production in your muscles, helps you push out 1-2 extra reps, speeds recovery between sets, and adds about 2-5 pounds of lean muscle over 8-12 weeks when combined with training.

I noticed the difference within two weeks. My working sets on bench press went from 8 reps to 10 reps at the same weight. My recovery between heavy squat sessions improved. These aren't massive gains, but when you're building muscle after 35, every advantage matters.

The dose? 5 grams daily. That's one teaspoon. Costs about $15 for a three-month supply of Optimum Nutrition Micronized Creatine Monohydrate — this is the one I buy, unflavored, mixes fine in my morning coffee.

The HCL Marketing Pitch vs Reality

Creatine HCL manufacturers make three main claims. Let me break down each one based on what I experienced and what the data shows.

Claim 1: "You only need 750mg instead of 5g!"

This is technically true because HCL has better solubility. But here's the thing: absorption isn't the limiting factor with monohydrate. Your body absorbs nearly all of the 5g you take. The "smaller dose" benefit is purely about convenience and marketing differentiation.

Claim 2: "No bloating or stomach issues!"

I never experienced bloating with monohydrate. Neither did most guys I know. The bloating thing became an issue because people were doing "loading phases" of 20g per day in the '90s. Skip the loading phase, take 5g daily, and you're fine. I've been doing this for three years.

If you're one of the rare guys who gets stomach issues from monohydrate, sure, try HCL. But don't assume you need it.

Claim 3: "Better absorption means better results!"

Show me the study where HCL produced better strength or muscle gains than monohydrate. I'll wait. It doesn't exist. The theoretical absorption advantage doesn't translate to better results in the gym.

What Actually Matters for Muscle Building After 35

Creatine is useful, but it's maybe 3% of the equation. Here's what matters more:

Progressive overload. I track every workout in a basic notebook. If I hit 3 sets of 8 reps at 185 pounds on bench press, next week I'm trying for 3 sets of 9, or adding 5 pounds and going for 3 sets of 6. This matters more than any supplement.

Protein intake. I eat about 160-180g of protein daily at 185 pounds bodyweight. Greek yogurt, chicken thighs, eggs, and a protein shake on training days. Your muscles need building blocks. Creatine just helps you train harder.

Sleep. I sleep 7.5 hours per night. When I don't, my lifts suffer immediately. Creatine can't fix sleep deprivation.

I also take vitamin D3 (5,000 IU daily) and magnesium glycinate (400mg before bed) because most guys over 35 are deficient. These improved my recovery and sleep quality more noticeably than creatine did.

The Practical Difference: My 6-Month Test

I spent three months on monohydrate, took a one-month washout period, then three months on HCL. Same workout plan (push/pull/legs split, three days per week). Same calorie intake. Same everything.

Monohydrate results:

  • Added 15 pounds to my deadlift (315 to 330)
  • Gained 4 pounds (some water, some muscle)
  • Zero side effects
  • Cost: $15

HCL results:

  • Added 10 pounds to my deadlift (330 to 340)
  • Gained 2 pounds
  • Zero side effects
  • Cost: $120

The HCL period came second, so you'd expect slightly better gains from the continued training. Instead, the results were nearly identical. The only real difference was my bank account.

What I Actually Recommend

Buy creatine monohydrate. Take 5g every single day, even on rest days. Mix it in your morning coffee, protein shake, or just water. Timing doesn't matter — I used to stress about "post-workout windows" but consistency matters way more than timing.

Give it 4-6 weeks before judging results. You won't suddenly look like you're on steroids. You'll add a rep here, five pounds there. Over months, that compounds into real muscle and strength gains.

If you're doing a home workout no equipment routine, creatine still helps. It improves performance on high-rep bodyweight exercises too. I noticed the difference on push-ups and pull-ups.

Skip the HCL unless you've tried monohydrate and had genuine stomach issues. Even then, try micronized monohydrate first — it's more finely ground and easier to digest.

The supplement industry wants you buying the newest, most expensive option. I fell for it. You don't have to.

Next steps: If you're not taking creatine yet, add it this week. If you're taking HCL, finish your current bottle, then switch to monohydrate and use that saved money for better groceries. Your 38-year-old self (or whatever age you're chasing) will thank you for spending smart and training hard.
