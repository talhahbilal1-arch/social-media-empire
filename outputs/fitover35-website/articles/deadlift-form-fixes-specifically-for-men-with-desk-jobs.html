5 Deadlift Form Fixes for Desk Jobs (#2 Saved My Back)

I spent six years sitting in an office chair for 9 hours a day before I figured out why my deadlifts felt like garbage. My hips were tight, my glutes forgot how to fire, and every time I pulled heavy, my lower back did all the work.

Sound familiar?

Here's the thing nobody tells you: sitting all day doesn't just make you weak—it fundamentally changes how your body moves. Your hip flexors shorten, your hamstrings get stiff, and your glutes basically clock out for the day around 10 AM.

Then you walk into the gym and try to deadlift like an athlete who's been moving all day. No wonder your back hurts.

I fixed my deadlift at 36 after dealing with this exact problem. These aren't generic form cues you've heard a thousand times. These are specific adjustments for guys whose bodies have adapted to sitting.

Fix #1: The 90/90 Hip Reset Before You Even Touch the Bar

Your hip flexors are locked short from sitting. You can't get into a proper hip hinge position until you fix this.

Before every deadlift session, I spend 5 minutes in the 90/90 position. Sit on the floor with one leg in front (90-degree angle) and one behind (also 90 degrees). Lean forward over the front leg for 60 seconds, then switch sides. Do this three times per side.

I know it looks weird. I don't care. This single drill took my deadlift from "feels like my spine is compressing" to "actually using my hips."

The difference is immediate. Your first warm-up set will feel smoother because your hips can actually hinge instead of your lower back compensating for tight hip flexors.

Fix #2: Stop Setting Up Like a Powerlifter (Use the Desk Job Setup Instead)

This is the one that saved my back.

Standard deadlift advice says to keep the bar over mid-foot, drop your hips, chest up. Great—except when your hip mobility sucks from sitting all day, you can't actually get into that position without rounding your lower back.

Here's what I do now: I set up with the bar slightly closer to my shins (almost touching), and I start with my hips HIGHER than you'd think. My back angle is more horizontal. It looks less "textbook," but my spine stays neutral and my hamstrings actually engage.

For men over 35 with desk jobs, this higher-hip setup is safer and stronger. You're working with your body's current limitations instead of forcing a position you can't maintain.

I pulled 315 for reps once I stopped trying to look like Instagram deadlifters and started working with my actual hip mobility.

Fix #3: Activate Your Glutes or Your Back Will Do Their Job

Your glutes are asleep. They've been sitting in a chair getting blood flow cut off for years.

Dead serious—if you don't wake them up before deadlifting, your lower back and hamstrings will take over the lift entirely. That's why your back is always sore and your glutes never grow.

My warm-up: 3 sets of 20 glute bridges with a 2-second squeeze at the top. Then 2 sets of 10 single-leg Romanian deadlifts with just your bodyweight. Focus on squeezing your glute at the top of each rep.

This takes 4 minutes. It's added 40 pounds to my working sets because my glutes actually participate in the lift now.

Fix #4: Bracing for Guys With Weak Cores From Sitting

Sitting all day weakens your core's ability to create intra-abdominal pressure. You think you're bracing, but you're actually just holding your breath while your spine flexes.

Here's how I actually learned to brace: Before you pull, take a deep breath into your belly (not your chest). Push your abs out against your belt like you're trying to make your stomach as big as possible. Hold that pressure through the entire rep.

Practice this with just the bar for a week. Seriously. I spent two weeks doing sets of 5 with 135 pounds, focusing only on maintaining that belly pressure. My working weight jumped 30 pounds the next month because my spine finally had real support.

If you're doing a workout plan over 35, core stability matters more than it did at 25. Your injury recovery time is longer now—prevention is everything.

Fix #5: The Desk Job Hinge Pattern Drill

Most guys with desk jobs don't actually know how to hip hinge anymore. We've been flexing at the spine to pick things up for years.

Here's the drill I used to relearn the pattern: Stand with your back against a wall. Put a broomstick against your spine—it should touch your head, upper back, and tailbone. Now push your hips back while keeping all three contact points on the stick.

That's a real hip hinge. Your knees barely bend. Your shins stay vertical. Your lower back doesn't round.

Do 3 sets of 10 of these every morning for two weeks. I'm not exaggerating—this drill reprogrammed my movement pattern more than any amount of deadlifting with bad form ever did.

Once you can do this perfectly, practice it with a kettlebell. Then practice it with a barbell. Then add weight.

Building muscle after 35 requires patience with movement patterns. You can't muscle through bad mechanics anymore.

The Reality Check

You're not going to deadlift like a 22-year-old athlete who's never had a desk job. Your body has adapted to sitting. That's not an excuse—it's reality.

These fixes work because they respect where your body actually is right now. I went from deadlifting 225 with lower back pain to pulling 365 for reps at 38 by accepting that I needed a different approach.

Start with these fixes next session. Pick two of them—don't try to implement everything at once. The 90/90 hip reset and the glute activation made the biggest difference for me personally.

Your back will thank you. And your deadlift numbers will finally start moving again.
