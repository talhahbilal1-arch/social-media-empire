Vitamin D and Testosterone: Does Supplementing Actually Boost Your Levels?

I started taking vitamin D three years ago after bloodwork showed I was at 22 ng/mL—basically deficient. My doctor mentioned it might help with testosterone, but I was skeptical. Another supplement promising miracles? Sure.

Six months later at 52 ng/mL, my total testosterone went from 487 to 623 ng/dL. Same diet, same training. The only variable was vitamin D.

Here's what the actual research shows, what dosage worked for me, and whether you should care.

The Research: What Studies Actually Found

A 2011 study from Hormone and Metabolic Research tracked 165 overweight men for a year. Half got 3,332 IU of vitamin D daily, half got placebo. The vitamin D group increased total testosterone by 25% and free testosterone by 20%. The placebo group? No change.

Another study from the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology examined 1,362 men and found a clear correlation: guys with vitamin D levels above 30 ng/mL had significantly higher testosterone than those below 20 ng/mL. We're talking roughly 150-180 ng/dL difference on average.

But here's where it gets specific—vitamin D only seemed to boost testosterone in men who were deficient to begin with. If you're already at 50+ ng/mL, taking more D3 won't magically spike your testosterone into superhuman territory.

I've tracked my bloodwork quarterly for three years now. At 22 ng/mL, my testosterone was suboptimal. At 52 ng/mL, it normalized. At 68 ng/mL (I experimented with higher doses), testosterone stayed basically the same as at 52.

Why Vitamin D Affects Testosterone (The Biology Part)

Vitamin D isn't actually a vitamin—it's a steroid hormone. Your body has vitamin D receptors in the testes, and research suggests D3 might help regulate the enzymes that convert cholesterol into testosterone.

Think of it this way: if you're deficient, you're trying to build muscle with half the construction crew showing up. Supplementing brings the full crew back.

I'm not a doctor, but my understanding from conversations with my endocrinologist is this: vitamin D deficiency creates a hormonal environment where testosterone production gets compromised. Fix the deficiency, normalize the environment, testosterone follows.

Most guys over 35 are deficient. A 2017 study found that 42% of American adults have insufficient vitamin D levels. If you work indoors, live north of Atlanta, or wear sunscreen religiously, you're probably in that group.

The Dosage That Actually Worked

I started at 5,000 IU daily after that initial bloodwork. Three months later, I retested: 38 ng/mL. Better, but still not optimal. I bumped to 7,000 IU daily for another three months and hit 52 ng/mL.

That's where I've stayed for three years now. Maintenance dose is 5,000 IU daily during winter (I live in Chicago), 3,000 IU during summer when I'm outside more.

The clinical recommendation is to aim for blood levels between 40-60 ng/mL. Below 30 is insufficient. Below 20 is deficient. Above 100 can cause toxicity, though that's rare and usually requires doses above 10,000 IU daily for extended periods.

Here's what I learned the hard way: you cannot gauge your vitamin D status by how you feel. I felt completely normal at 22 ng/mL. No fatigue, no obvious symptoms. The only way to know is bloodwork.

Get a 25-hydroxyvitamin D blood test. It costs about $50 without insurance, sometimes less. Most doctors will order it if you ask, especially if you mention concerns about testosterone or muscle building after 35.

The Other Factors You Can't Ignore

Vitamin D alone won't save you if you're sleeping 5 hours a night, eating garbage, and never lifting heavy. Testosterone optimization is multifactorial.

What actually works for men over 35 fitness:

  • Resistance training 3-4x weekly focusing on compound movements (squats, deadlifts, bench press)
  • 7-8 hours of sleep consistently—this matters more than any supplement
  • Body fat under 20%—higher body fat increases aromatase enzyme activity, converting testosterone to estrogen
  • Adequate protein (0.8-1g per pound of body weight if you're training)

What I'd Do If I Started Over

I'd get bloodwork first. Know your baseline vitamin D and testosterone before changing anything. This isn't optional—guessing wastes time and money.

Start with 5,000 IU daily of vitamin D3 if you're deficient. Take it with breakfast or your largest meal. Retest in 12 weeks. Adjust dosage based on results, aiming for 40-60 ng/mL.

Track your training and body weight weekly. Vitamin D works better when you're actually building muscle and managing body fat. You can't supplement your way out of a shitty lifestyle.

Consider the supporting factors: magnesium for sleep, protein for muscle building after 35, resistance training for the hormonal response. Vitamin D is the base layer, not the entire strategy.

And be patient. My testosterone didn't jump overnight. It took six months of consistent supplementing, training, and lifestyle improvements. The research shows similar timelines—meaningful testosterone changes happen over months, not weeks.
