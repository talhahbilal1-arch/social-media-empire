5 Rucking Changes That Shocked Me After 40: Real Training Results

I bought a weighted backpack on a Tuesday. By Thursday, I was convinced I'd made a terrible mistake.

My lower back felt like I'd moved furniture all day. My traps were screaming. And I'd only walked around the block with 20 pounds.

But I'm stubborn. Three months later, I'm carrying 45 pounds for 5 miles, my knees feel better than they have in years, and I'm eating more while losing fat. Rucking became the home workout no equipment approach I didn't know I needed—just add weight to a pack and walk.

Here's what actually happened when I committed to rucking as a men over 35 fitness strategy.

Started With 20 Pounds, Hit 45 Pounds in 12 Weeks—Here's the Timeline

I see guys throw 40 pounds in a pack on day one. Don't do that. I learned this the hard way after that first brutal week.

Week 1-2: Started with 20 pounds (two 10-lb dumbbells in an old backpack). Walked 2 miles, three times per week. Felt awkward and heavy. Questioned everything.

Week 3-4: Same 20 pounds, but bumped to 3 miles. My body stopped fighting it. Started looking forward to these walks.

Week 5-6: Added 5 pounds. This is where I made my first mistake—I jumped to 30 pounds too fast. Backed off to 25 pounds instead. Much better.

Week 7-9: Comfortable at 25 pounds for 3-4 miles. Added a fourth ruck per week, keeping one short (2 miles) and easy.

Week 10-12: Hit 35 pounds. Then 40. Finally 45. The progression felt natural because I didn't rush it.

The key for muscle building after 35: add weight only when the current load feels easy for your full distance. If you're grinding through the last mile, you're not ready to add more.

I grabbed a proper weighted vest around week 8—the weight distribution is way better than a backpack once you're serious about this.

My Rear Delts Actually Became Visible

I've done overhead press, lateral raises, and every shoulder workout you can name. Nothing made my rear delts pop like rucking did.

Around week 6, my wife pointed at my shoulder from behind and asked if I'd been doing something different. I hadn't even noticed. The back of my shoulders had this defined look I'd never achieved with traditional lifting.
