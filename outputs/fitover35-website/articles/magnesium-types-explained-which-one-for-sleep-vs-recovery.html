The Magnesium Maze: Different Types Explained

Here's the deal: not all magnesium is created equal. You see "magnesium" on the label, but what kind of magnesium is it bonded to? That makes all the difference in terms of absorption and what it does in your body.

  • Magnesium Glycinate: This is the rockstar for sleep. Glycine is a calming amino acid, and when combined with magnesium, it becomes a potent relaxant. It's also highly absorbable, which is crucial. This is the type I use every night, and it's been a game-changer.
  • Magnesium Oxide: Avoid this one like the plague. It's cheap and poorly absorbed. You’ll mostly be flushing it down the toilet (literally), and it's more likely to cause digestive issues than improve anything. Seriously, don’t waste your money.
  • Magnesium Citrate: This is often used as a laxative. While it's absorbed okay, it can cause digestive upset in some people. Not ideal before bed, unless you want to be making midnight bathroom trips.
  • Magnesium Threonate: This is a newer form that shows promise for cognitive function and brain health. It may also help with sleep, but it's more expensive, and the research is still emerging. I haven’t personally experimented with this one enough to offer a strong recommendation.
  • Magnesium Sulfate (Epsom Salts): While you don't ingest this one, soaking in Epsom salts is a great way to absorb magnesium through your skin and relax sore muscles after a tough workout. I often add Epsom salts to a hot bath, especially after a leg day.

Magnesium for Muscle Recovery: What Works Best

Beyond sleep, magnesium plays a vital role in muscle function and recovery. A deficiency can lead to muscle cramps, fatigue, and even impaired performance in your home workout no equipment. I noticed this firsthand when I was pushing hard to lose belly fat men. I thought I was just getting old and beat up, but turns out, my magnesium levels were low.

For recovery, I still lean towards magnesium glycinate](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B000BD0RT0?tag=dailydealdarling1-20) because it helps with muscle relaxation and reduces inflammation. However, I also incorporate Epsom salt baths a few times a week. It's a simple and effective way to soothe sore muscles and boost magnesium levels. Adding something like fish oil](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B004O2I9JO?tag=dailydealdarling1-20) to your daily stack can also greatly improve recovery times. I have found that the two used together have had the most impact on my recovery times!

> Pro-Coach Insight: Try taking magnesium glycinate about an hour before bed on an empty stomach for optimal absorption. Start with a dose of 200-400mg. If you experience any digestive upset, reduce the dose. You might also experiment with magnesium citrate if the glycinate doesn't work for you; however, monitor how your body reacts.

Dosage and Timing: Dialing It In

Okay, let’s get practical. I typically take 400mg of magnesium glycinate an hour before bed. I find that this dose helps me relax without making me feel groggy in the morning. However, everyone's different, so you may need to adjust the dosage to find what works best for you.

Regarding timing, consistency is key. Taking magnesium every night helps maintain consistent levels in your body and allows you to experience its benefits over time. Also, consider the other supplements you're taking. Some supplements, like calcium, can interfere with magnesium absorption. If you're taking calcium, take it at a different time of day than your magnesium.

Is Magnesium Right for You?

Before you run out and buy a truckload of magnesium, it's worth considering whether you're actually deficient. Symptoms of magnesium deficiency can include muscle cramps, fatigue, insomnia, and irritability. However, these symptoms can also be caused by other factors.

If you suspect you're deficient, talk to your doctor. They can order a blood test to check your magnesium levels. Keep in mind, though, that blood tests aren't always accurate because most magnesium is stored in your bones and tissues, not in your blood.

Even if you're not officially deficient, supplementing with magnesium can still be beneficial, especially if you're active or under a lot of stress. As men over 35 fitness becomes more of a focus, don't forget about all the little things!

Pros vs Cons: Magnesium Glycinate vs. Magnesium Oxide

Magnesium Oxide

  • Inexpensive

  • Cons:
  • Poorly absorbed
  • Likely to cause digestive upset
  • Less effective for sleep and relaxation
