# Objection-Handling Playbook for Freelance Web Design
## Complete Website Redesign with Conversion Optimization | $3,500 Project Fee | Solopreneurs & Small Business Owners

---

## OBJECTION 1: "That's more than I was expecting to spend"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They've either been shopping around and seen cheaper designers, or they're underestimating what a conversion-optimized redesign actually costs.

**REFRAME:** The cost isn't about the design — it's about the revenue recovery from fixing what's currently broken.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"I appreciate you saying that. (pause) Most people expect to pay less until we talk about what your current site is actually costing you. If your site is converting at 1.5% and we move it to 2.5%, that's an extra conversion per week for most businesses. At your average order value, that's real revenue we're talking about. The redesign pays for itself in the first month or two. What's your current conversion rate, and what do you think you're losing because people bounce?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** What percentage of visitors become customers right now, and how much revenue are you leaving on the table?

---

## OBJECTION 2: "I need to think about it for a few days"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They need time to justify the expense, or they're comparing you to other designers and want to see all options first.

**REFRAME:** Every day your current site is live, it's sending people away — thinking about it doesn't fix that.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"That's fine, and I get the instinct. (pause) Here's what happens though: most people who say this end up not moving forward because the urgency fades. Your site isn't getting better while you're thinking. It's actively losing customers. What I'd suggest is this: what's the actual concern? Is it the investment amount, or are you trying to decide between me and other designers? Because if it's the latter, I'd rather you know exactly what you're getting."

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** Is the hesitation about the investment, or are you comparing options?

---

## OBJECTION 3: "I need to run this by my [partner/spouse]"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They want external validation, or they're avoiding a solo decision on spending.

**REFRAME:** Frame it for your partner in terms of what it means for the business, not the cost.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"That makes sense. (pause) When you talk to them, here's how I'd frame it: we're fixing the number one reason people aren't buying — your site isn't clearly showing them what we do or making it easy to take the next step. The redesign focuses on moving visitors from 'I'm browsing' to 'I want to buy.' What matters most to your partner: is it the impact on customers, the timeline, or something else? Let me know and I can address it directly."

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** What's your partner's main concern — timeline, the investment, or proof this will actually convert better?

---

## OBJECTION 4: "I tried something similar before and it didn't work for me"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They hired a designer before who either didn't deliver or created something that looked good but didn't convert.

**REFRAME:** Most design failures happen because the designer didn't care about conversion — they cared about aesthetics.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"I want to understand what happened. (pause) A lot of business owners have hired designers who made the site pretty but forgot to ask: 'Will this actually convert?' I'm guessing your last site looked fine but the visitors still weren't turning into customers. That's because most designers think about design first and business results second. What I do is flip that: I design around getting you sales. Everything is measured against whether it moves someone closer to a purchase. What specifically didn't work about your last redesign?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** Did the last design fail because it looked wrong, or because people weren't buying?

---

## OBJECTION 5: "I can probably figure this out myself with free resources"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They're trying to avoid spending money, or they've used website builders and think they can do it again.

**REFRAME:** Free website builders get you a site; design strategy gets you conversions.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"You could build something with Wix or Squarespace, sure. (pause) But here's what I've seen: DIY sites look like DIY sites. Visitors can feel the difference. More importantly, the structure of the site — where the CTA goes, how you guide them through your story, what objections you address — that's what separates sites that convert from sites that look pretty. You could spend 40 hours learning design software, or you could spend 3,500 dollars and get something that earns itself back in conversions. What's your hourly rate, and how many hours would you actually spend trying to build something professional?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** If you invested those hours into actually running your business instead, what could you accomplish?

---

## OBJECTION 6: "This sounds great but the timing isn't right"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They're either cash-constrained, or they don't feel urgent about the redesign because business feels okay right now.

**REFRAME:** The longer you wait, the more conversions you're missing — and the more your competitors are pulling ahead.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"I understand, timing can feel tight. (pause) What I'll point out is that every month you're waiting, your site is converting at whatever your current rate is. If we started today, you'd be capturing customers 30 days sooner than if you wait. Your competitors aren't waiting to improve their sites. What would need to shift for you to make this happen in the next 2 weeks?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** What would need to happen financially for this to work in the next 30 days?

---

## OBJECTION 7: "Can you just email me the details?"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They want to review independently, or they're using email as a barrier to avoid commitment.

**REFRAME:** A proposal without understanding your specific business isn't actually useful — details matter less than strategy.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"I can absolutely send something, but here's the thing: a generic proposal doesn't address your specific conversion problem. (pause) What would actually be useful is if we spent 15 minutes talking about your visitors, what's holding them back, and exactly what I'd fix. Then I send you something that's built for *your* business. Would it make sense to map that out first?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** Can you tell me what your #1 complaint is from visitors — do they not understand your offer, not trust you, or just find it hard to buy?

---

## OBJECTION 8: "I'm already working with someone on this"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They have an existing designer or are in process with another one.

**REFRAME:** If the existing designer was delivering conversion-focused work, you probably wouldn't be open to alternatives.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"I appreciate you telling me that. (pause) I'm curious what that's been like — are they focused on conversions, or is it more of a design update? Because in my experience, a lot of designers deliver beautiful sites that don't move visitors to actually buy. That's different work than what I do. What's your main concern with the redesign you're currently working on?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** Is the redesign tracking toward your conversion goals, or is it more of an aesthetic update?

---

## OBJECTION 9: "How do I know this will actually work for my specific situation?"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They're afraid of spending money and seeing no improvement in conversions.

**REFRAME:** The work is structured so we can see progress in the right direction before we launch.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"That's a smart question to ask. (pause) Here's how we de-risk this: we do the redesign in phases. We build the new site in a staging environment, test the copy and layout with your actual traffic, and measure conversions before it goes live. If the metrics show improvement, we launch. If something isn't working, we adjust before the world sees it. You don't pay full price until you see the conversion data backs it up. What would an improvement look like for you — 20% better conversion rate, more email signups, faster checkout?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** What specific conversion metric would prove to you that the new design is working?

---

## OBJECTION 10: "What happens if I'm not happy with the results?"

**HIDDEN MEANING:** They want to know there's recourse if the redesign doesn't deliver better conversions.

**REFRAME:** If we've done our testing right, you'll already know it works before the project is final.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**
"I want to be direct here: if you're not happy, we iterate until you are. (pause) But — and this is important — most of the time the problem isn't the design, it's that we misunderstood your customer. So before we redesign anything, we do a deep dive into who's visiting your site and why they're not converting. If we nail that, the design improvement follows. By the time we're ready to launch, you've already seen the conversion data showing it works. That's the confidence I build the price around. Does that approach make sense for you?"

**BRIDGE QUESTION:** Would it help to see the data and user testing results before we finalize the design?

---

## CLOSING SECTION: The 5-Step Warm Close

**Step 1: Summarize the 3 things they said they wanted**

"Let me make sure I've got this right. You said three things matter: you want a site that actually converts visitors into customers, you want it done right so you don't have to redo it again, and you want to understand why it's working before committing. Is that the picture?"

**Step 2: Directly address their one remaining concern**

"The one thing I'm picking up on is you want proof it'll work for your specific business. That's exactly why we test before launch — we'll show you the conversion data, the user testing, everything that proves the new design is an upgrade. You'll see it working before you pay final invoice."

**Step 3: Present two options (not "yes or no" but Option A or Option B)**

"Here's how we move forward: Option A is we start this week — I'll run a quick audit of your current site, identify the conversion killers, and send you a detailed plan by Thursday. Then we move into the design phase. Option B is we schedule for next month, but you'll have 30 more days of conversions at your current rate. Which timeline works for your business?"

**Step 4: State the simple next step in one sentence**

"If you want to start this week, I'll send you the audit scope and we'll lock in your project kickoff for Monday."

**Step 5: Be silent and wait (explain why silence matters here)**

You've given them two paths forward, both require action, neither is "think about it." The silence after this is where they make the real decision. They're not deciding between redesign or no redesign — you've already sold that. They're deciding between starting now or starting later. When they feel the pause, they'll either commit to a timeline or ask what's blocking them from committing. Either way, the conversation moves from thinking to deciding, and that's where you want it. Silence respects their judgment as a business owner — you're not pushing, you're expecting them to know what they need. Most people respect that and choose to move forward.

---

*This playbook works because it acknowledges the real fear: spending money on design and getting nothing in return. You're not dismissing that fear — you're showing exactly how you prevent it. The tone is supportive and grounded because you've rebuilt enough sites to know what works and what doesn't. You're not overselling; you're sharing what you've learned. That confidence is what converts hesitation into commitment.*
