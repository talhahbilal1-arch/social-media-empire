# How The Planner Works

The planner runs on twenty templates. That is the entire system.

A template is a meal structure with three locked elements: a category of protein, a category of carbohydrate, and a category of fat or vegetable. Inside each category you can swap freely. The macro profile holds within roughly five grams of protein, ten grams of carbs, and three grams of fat per meal as long as you stay within the template's portion guide.

Twenty templates sounds small. It is supposed to. The buyer who needs this planner does not need 200 recipes. He needs a small set of structures he can run on autopilot and adapt to whatever the week throws at him.

## The four template families

| Family | Templates | Purpose |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Breakfast | 4 | Hit 30-50g protein in under 10 minutes |
| Lunch | 6 | Portable, prep-friendly, 40-60g protein |
| Dinner | 6 | Cooked from scratch or reheated, 40-60g protein |
| Snacks | 4 | Fill protein gaps, manage hunger between meals |

Every template carries a macro profile at a "standard portion" plus three scale-up rules: how to push it to a 40g protein hit, a 60g hit, or an 80g hit. You pick the scale based on your daily target and how the rest of the day looks.

## Why templates over recipes

Three reasons.

**1. Templates handle substitutions automatically.** If the recipe calls for chicken and you have ground turkey, a recipe blows up. A template does not. Both proteins live in the same category with similar macros per ounce. You swap, you eat, you move on.

**2. Templates teach you food, not steps.** After a month of running Lunch Template 3, you know what 35g of protein looks like on a plate. You stop weighing every chicken breast. The kitchen scale comes out for new foods only.

**3. Templates survive the grocery store.** When chicken thighs are $2.99 a pound and chicken breast is $7.49, a recipe locks you in. A template lets you buy what is on sale and slot it in.

## The portion principle

Every template assumes you are using one of three standard portion sizes for the protein:

- Small: 4 oz cooked weight (about 28-32g protein for most lean meats)
- Medium: 6 oz cooked weight (42-48g protein)
- Large: 8 oz cooked weight (56-64g protein)

Carbs and fats scale similarly. A "standard" carb portion is one cup cooked rice, one medium potato, two slices of bread, or one large tortilla. A "standard" fat portion is one tablespoon of oil or nut butter, half an avocado, or one ounce of cheese.

Once you know what those portions look like by eye, you stop measuring most things. The scale stays on the counter for accountability, but you use it three times a week, not eighteen.

## The rotation rule

Pick a short rotation and stay in it for two weeks. Three breakfasts, four lunches, four dinners, two snacks. Eleven templates total in the active set. That is enough variety to not hate your life and few enough that you can grocery shop on autopilot.

After two weeks, swap one template at a time. Never rebuild the whole rotation in one week. The point of a rotation is that it removes decisions, not that it gives you new ones.

## What this does not cover

The planner does not cover allergies, intolerances, or specific medical diets. If you are dairy-free, the Greek yogurt templates do not work as written. Swap to a fortified soy yogurt with similar protein per ounce and the math holds. The planner gives you the structure; your dietary constraints set the inputs.

It also does not cover competition prep, weight cuts under 7 days out, or any context that requires gram-precise accuracy. Templates are accurate enough for body recomposition. They are not accurate enough for an athlete dropping a weight class on Friday.
