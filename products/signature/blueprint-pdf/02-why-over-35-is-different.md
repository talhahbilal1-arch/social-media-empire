# Chapter 2: Why Over 35 Is Different

If you are reading this book, you have probably noticed that the things that used to work no longer work as well. The 1500-calorie cut that stripped fat in three weeks at age 27 now barely moves the scale at 42. The five-day bro split you ran in college now leaves your elbows aching by Wednesday. Sleep that used to feel optional now feels like a tax you pay every morning if you skipped it.

This is not in your head. The body changes after 35, and the changes are gradual enough that most men do not notice until they have lost ground they did not know they were losing. This chapter explains the four major shifts that matter for training and nutrition, and what they mean for how you should program your work.

## Hormonal Shifts

Total testosterone declines roughly 1 percent per year after age 30 in most men, and free testosterone often declines faster because sex hormone binding globulin tends to rise with age. This is well documented. What it means in practice is that recovery from heavy training takes longer, muscle protein synthesis in response to a given dose of protein is slightly blunted, and the margin for error in lifestyle factors that further suppress testosterone (poor sleep, chronic alcohol, excess body fat, undereating) shrinks considerably.

The practical implication is not that you need testosterone replacement therapy. For most men in the normal range, the answer is to stop doing the things that further suppress an already lower baseline. Sleep seven to nine hours. Keep body fat in a reasonable range, generally under 20 percent. Eat enough protein. Lift heavy enough to demand a hormonal response. These are the levers you actually control.

Growth hormone and IGF-1 also decline with age, which contributes to slower tissue repair. Insulin sensitivity tends to drift downward, particularly in men who have added body fat around the midsection.

## Slower Recovery

At 25, you could train a body part to failure on Monday and feel fresh by Wednesday. At 45, that same session may take five to seven days to fully recover from. This is partially hormonal and partially structural: connective tissue remodeling slows, the inflammatory response after high-volume training takes longer to resolve, and the central nervous system needs more time between true maximum-effort sessions.

This is why the program in this book is built around three training days per week instead of five or six. More training is not better when recovery is the limiting factor. Three high-quality sessions with progressive overload will outperform five mediocre sessions every time, and they will do it without breaking you down.

The other implication: you cannot push every set to failure on every exercise the way you might have at 22. Most working sets in this program target an RPE of 7 to 8, meaning you leave 2 to 3 reps in reserve. You will train hard, but you will train with margin.

## Joint Degeneration

Cartilage thins. Tendons get stiffer. The discs in the lumbar spine lose hydration. Rotator cuff tissue becomes less tolerant of high-volume overhead pressing. None of this is catastrophic in itself. It does mean that exercise selection matters more after 35 than it did before.

This is why the program uses trap bar deadlifts instead of conventional from-the-floor pulls for most lifters, why we prioritize the safety bar squat or front squat for those with cranky shoulders, and why dumbbell pressing variations replace heavy barbell bench for many sessions. None of these are inferior choices. They are choices that respect the tissue you have to train with.

A daily 10-minute mobility routine, covered in Chapter 7, addresses much of the stiffness that accumulates from desk work and aging. It is not optional.

## Insulin Sensitivity and Body Composition

Research consistently shows that insulin sensitivity declines with age, and the decline accelerates with weight gain, particularly visceral fat around the abdomen. Lower insulin sensitivity makes it easier to add fat and harder to lose it at any given calorie intake.

The two things that improve insulin sensitivity most reliably are resistance training and walking. Both are central to this program. Cardio for fat loss in this book is mostly walking, eight thousand steps per day minimum, because it is the most sustainable, lowest-cost, lowest-recovery-impact form of activity available, and it directly improves the metabolic environment you are trying to fix.

## What This Means for Your Training

The program in Chapter 4 reflects all of the above. Three days per week. Compound lifts with joint-friendly variations. Most working sets at RPE 7-8. A four-week wave of progression, then a deload. Walking baked in. Mobility daily.

You are not training like a 25-year-old, and you should not want to. You are training like a man who plans to still be lifting at 65, with shoulders and knees that work, and a body composition he is proud of.
