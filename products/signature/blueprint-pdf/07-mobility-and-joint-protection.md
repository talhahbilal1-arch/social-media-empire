# Chapter 7: Mobility and Joint Protection

Mobility is the pillar most men skip and most men regret skipping. The cost of skipping is invisible at first. You feel a little tighter on Tuesday than you used to. By month three, your hips do not rotate like they did. By year two, you cannot squat below parallel without your lower back rounding. By year five, you have a chronic shoulder issue that limits your bench press and your overhead press.

The cost of doing the work is ten minutes a day. That is the entire trade.

## What Mobility Is and Is Not

Mobility is active range of motion: how far you can move a joint under your own control. Flexibility is passive range of motion: how far the joint can be moved by an outside force. The two are related but not the same. A lifter can be very flexible (touches toes, full splits) but have terrible mobility (cannot control end-range positions). A man over 35 needs both, but mobility is what protects you under the bar.

Stretching alone, particularly long static stretches before training, does not build mobility. The routine in this chapter combines controlled articular rotations, dynamic positions, and end-range strength work. It builds usable range, not just looser tissue.

## The Daily 10-Minute Routine

Do this every day. Yes, every day. It takes ten minutes. You will not get measurable gains from doing it twice a week. Daily is the minimum effective dose because the joints respond to frequent low-volume input far better than infrequent high-volume input.

Best done in the morning or as part of your warm-up before training. On rest days, in front of the television is fine.

### The Routine

| Movement | Reps/Time | Focus |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Cat-cow | 8 slow reps | Spine segmentation, thoracic mobility |
| 90/90 hip switches | 8 per side | Internal and external hip rotation |
| Thoracic rotations on all fours | 8 per side | Mid-back rotation, shoulder mobility |
| World's greatest stretch | 4 per side | Hips, ankles, thoracic combined |
| Shoulder CARs | 5 per side | Active shoulder range |
| Wall slides | 10 reps | Scapular control, overhead position |
| Ankle rocks against wall | 10 per side | Dorsiflexion |
| Deep squat hold with reach | 30 sec, alternating reach | Hip and ankle range |

Total time: 9-11 minutes once you know the movements.

### Movement Notes

**Cat-cow.** On hands and knees. Slowly arch the spine up (cat) then drop the belly and lift the head (cow). The point is segment-by-segment movement, not speed. Think of moving one vertebra at a time.

**90/90 hip switches.** Sit on the floor with one leg in front bent at 90 degrees, the other leg behind also at 90 degrees. Slowly rotate the torso to switch sides without using the hands to push off. If switching is impossible, work the position by leaning forward over the front leg for a stretch.

**Thoracic rotations on all fours.** On hands and knees. Place one hand behind the head. Rotate the elbow up toward the ceiling, following with the eyes. Return slowly. The motion should come from the mid-back, not the lower back.

**World's greatest stretch.** Step into a deep lunge. Place the same-side hand on the floor inside the front foot. Rotate the opposite arm up to the ceiling. Hold briefly, then return. This single movement opens hips, ankles, thoracic spine, and shoulder.

**Shoulder CARs.** Stand. Lift one arm, draw the largest possible circle with the hand, keeping the rest of the body still. The slower and more controlled, the better. The goal is to find your true active range, not to make it look big.

**Wall slides.** Stand with back, head, and arms against a wall. Arms in a "field goal" position. Slide arms up the wall while keeping contact. Most men will lose contact with the lower back or wrists at some point. That is the position to work on.

**Ankle rocks against wall.** Stand 4 inches from a wall. Drive the knee toward the wall over the toe without lifting the heel. Work the angle that finds full dorsiflexion.

**Deep squat hold with reach.** Squat as deep as you can, holding onto a doorframe or pole if needed for balance. Reach one arm up to the ceiling, alternating sides. The reach drives thoracic extension at the bottom of the squat.

## Pre-Workout Activation vs Static Stretching

The warm-up before training, described in Chapter 4, uses dynamic activation movements. This is intentional. The research on pre-workout static stretching is mixed at best. Long static holds (over 60 seconds) before lifting can acutely reduce force production and may slightly increase injury risk in heavy lifts.

Save static stretching for after training or for evenings on the couch, when you want passive range work without compromising the next set.

The pre-workout activation done correctly takes 8-10 minutes and prepares the joints, raises core temperature, and primes the nervous system. Skipping it to "save time" is the most expensive 8 minutes you will save in the gym.

## Joint Pain: Red Flags and When to See a Physiotherapist

Discomfort is normal in training. Pain is information. The ability to tell the two apart is a skill you should develop.

**Acceptable discomfort:**

- Muscle soreness 24-72 hours after a hard session, fading day by day
- Tightness or restriction in a specific position that loosens with the warm-up
- Mild fatigue ache during the last reps of a hard set

**See a physiotherapist within 1-2 weeks if:**

- Joint pain that persists for more than 7 days
- Pain that gets worse during a session rather than better with warm-up
- Sharp, stabbing, or electric pain anywhere
- Pain that wakes you at night
- Pain accompanied by significant swelling, bruising, or instability
- Pain during normal daily movement, not just under load
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in an arm or leg

**See a physiotherapist immediately if:**

- Sudden severe pain during a lift accompanied by a "pop" or "snap"
- Significant range-of-motion loss that does not improve over 48 hours
- Neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness) that persist or spread

A good physiotherapist is one of the best investments a man over 35 can make in his training longevity. Find one who treats lifters and works with you on returning to lifting, not one who tells you to stop training and rest. They exist. Ask other lifters in your area for referrals.

## The Honest Truth About Aging Joints

Some stiffness is age. Some restrictions you had at 25 are gone. The goal of this chapter is not to give you the mobility of a 22-year-old gymnast. It is to keep the range you have, modestly expand it where possible, and protect you from the slow decline that puts most men out of the gym in their fifties.

Ten minutes a day. For the next thirty years. That is the prescription.
