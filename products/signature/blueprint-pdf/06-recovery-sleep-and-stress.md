# Chapter 6: Recovery, Sleep, and Stress

If training is the stimulus and nutrition is the raw material, recovery is the construction crew. The crew works night shift. Skip enough nights and the building never gets built no matter how much steel you deliver to the site.

This is the chapter most men skim. Do not. The single biggest difference between a 25-year-old's response to training and a 45-year-old's response is recovery capacity. You cannot out-train poor sleep, and at your age, you cannot even maintain on it.

## Sleep: Why Seven to Nine Hours Matters More Now

The total sleep requirement for adult men does not shift dramatically with age. What shifts is how much of that sleep is restorative. Deep sleep (slow-wave sleep) and REM sleep both decline progressively with age. Deep sleep in particular, which is when most growth hormone is released and when much of the muscle repair work happens, can drop by 30-50 percent between age 25 and age 50 in men with otherwise normal sleep architecture.

This means two things in practice:

1. The total time you spend in bed matters more, because a smaller percentage of it is the high-value sleep stages.
2. The quality factors that protect deep sleep matter more: cool room, dark room, no late alcohol, no late caffeine, consistent timing.

Generally accepted research targets are 7 to 9 hours of total sleep per night for adults. For men over 35 in a hard training phase, target the upper end of that range. Eight hours is the practical floor for a recovery week.

## Sleep Hygiene Checklist

Treat this as a checklist, not a ritual. Hit most of them most nights.

| Item | Standard |
|------|----------|
| Bedroom temperature | 60-67 degrees F (15-19 C) |
| Light at sleep time | Dark enough you cannot see your hand at arm's length |
| Caffeine cutoff | 8-10 hours before bed (so 1-2 PM for a 10 PM sleep) |
| Alcohol cutoff | 3+ hours before bed; ideally none on training nights |
| Last meal | 2+ hours before bed for most men |
| Screen brightness | Reduce after sunset; use night mode on phone and laptop |
| Sleep timing | Same bedtime and wake time within 30 min, including weekends |
| Bedroom use | Sleep and sex only; no work in bed |

The single highest-leverage change for most men is consistent timing. The body's circadian system runs on schedule. Letting the schedule drift by two hours on weekends produces something closely resembling jet lag every Monday morning.

## HRV Basics

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Higher HRV generally indicates a well-recovered nervous system. Lower HRV generally indicates accumulated stress, poor sleep, alcohol the previous night, illness, or overtraining.

You do not need a wearable to use this concept. If you have one (Whoop, Oura, Garmin, Apple Watch), use it as a trend signal, not a daily oracle. A single low HRV morning means little. A 7-day average that is 15 percent below your baseline means you should consider reducing training intensity for a few days.

If you do not have a wearable, use the resting heart rate proxy. Take your pulse first thing in the morning, in bed, before you stand up. Do this for two weeks to establish your baseline. A morning resting heart rate 7-10 beats above baseline indicates the same thing: pull back, eat well, sleep more, train light or rest.

## Stress Management Tools That Actually Work

The point of this section is to give you tools you will actually use, not aspirational practices you will quit by week three. Pick two or three. Use them consistently.

**Box breathing.** Four seconds in, four seconds hold, four seconds out, four seconds hold. Repeat for 2-5 minutes. Useful before training to switch from work mode to focus mode, or in the evening to wind down. No app required.

**Walking.** A 20-minute walk lowers cortisol, clears the head, and improves insulin sensitivity in one session. Take walks during the workday rather than scheduling a single big walk you will skip. Two 10-minute walks beats one 20-minute walk you never start.

**The 4-7-8 breath for sleep onset.** Inhale 4 seconds through nose, hold 7 seconds, exhale 8 seconds through mouth. Four cycles. Useful when your mind is racing at bedtime.

**Daylight exposure within an hour of waking.** Five to ten minutes outside, preferably without sunglasses, anchors your circadian rhythm. This is among the most-evidenced low-effort interventions for sleep quality.

**Notes app dump before bed.** If you are lying in bed running through tomorrow's to-do list, write it down on your phone or a notepad. Removing it from working memory reliably reduces sleep onset time.

What is not on this list: 30-minute daily meditation. It works for some people. Most men in this demographic will not sustain it past week three. If you are already a meditator, continue. If not, do not start a habit you will not keep.

## Recovery Markers: How to Know You Are Recovered

These are the practical signals to watch:

- Morning resting heart rate within 5 beats of baseline
- Sleep felt restorative, not "the alarm yanked me out of unconsciousness"
- Last training session's soreness is gone or nearly gone
- You are looking forward to training, not dreading it
- Strength on warm-up sets feels normal or better

If three or more of these are off, take a true rest day, eat at maintenance or slightly above, sleep an extra hour, and revisit tomorrow.

## Off-Day Protocols

Off days are not zero days. They are low-stress, parasympathetic days. The protocol:

- 6,000-10,000 steps spread across the day
- One short mobility session (Chapter 7's routine)
- Full protein target hit (recovery happens whether you train or not)
- Sleep target hit
- Avoid additional high-intensity cardio, contact sports, or extra weights "just because"

The biggest mistake men make on off days is using them to do more cardio or extra accessory work because they "have the energy." That is the recovery you needed for Friday's main session. Spend it on the off day, lose it on Friday.

## Travel Weeks and Sick Weeks

When life disrupts the schedule, default to the minimum effective dose:

- Two short bodyweight or hotel-gym sessions of 30 minutes each
- Hit protein target every day
- Walk daily, target 8,000+ steps (easy when traveling)
- Sleep is harder; do what you can
- Drink less than you would have

This is a maintenance protocol, not a progression protocol. Your goal during a travel or sick week is to lose nothing, not to gain. Resume the program where you left off the week you return.

When you come back from a true illness (fever, more than 3 days down), restart at 80 percent of your previous loads for one session, then resume normal progression. Do not try to immediately match the weight you ended on. The body needs a ramp.
