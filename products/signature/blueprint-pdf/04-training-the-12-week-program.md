# Chapter 4: Training, the 12-Week Program

This is the load-bearing chapter of the book. Read all of it before you train a single session. The program is built as one connected arc across twelve weeks, with three phases of four weeks each. Knowing where you are headed in week 11 changes how you train in week 2.

## Program Overview

You will train three days per week, on non-consecutive days. The most common schedule is Monday, Wednesday, Friday, but Tuesday/Thursday/Saturday works equally well. What matters is at least one rest day between sessions and never more than two.

The split is A/B/C, rotating through three different sessions. Each session is full-body in nature but emphasizes a different primary movement pattern. This is not a body-part split. At your age and training frequency, full-body work outperforms isolation splits for hypertrophy and strength simultaneously.

The three phases:

| Phase | Weeks | Focus | Volume | Intensity |
|-------|-------|-------|--------|-----------|
| Foundation | 1-4 | Movement quality, base strength | Moderate | RPE 6-7 |
| Build | 5-8 | Hypertrophy, work capacity | High | RPE 7-8 |
| Intensify | 9-12 | Top-end strength, peaking | Moderate-low | RPE 8-9 |

Week 4, week 8, and week 12 each include a deload. Deload notes are in the relevant phase sections below.

## Equipment Requirements

You need access to: a barbell with plates, dumbbells up to at least 60 lbs per side, a flat bench, a power rack or squat stand with safety pins, a pull-up bar, and floor space. A trap bar (hex bar) is strongly recommended. A safety squat bar is a useful luxury but not required.

## Warm-up Protocol (every session)

Five minutes of low-intensity cardio (bike, row, or brisk walk on incline), followed by:

- Cat-cow, 8 reps
- 90/90 hip switches, 6 per side
- Thoracic rotations on all fours, 6 per side
- Shoulder CARs (controlled articular rotations), 5 per side
- Glute bridges, 10 reps
- Then two warm-up sets on your first compound lift, working up to your top weight

Total warm-up time: 8-10 minutes. Do not skip this. The warm-up is not optional padding; it is the difference between training and getting hurt.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

The goal of weeks 1-4 is movement quality and conservative loading. Most lifters under-load this phase and over-load week 5. Resist that.

For all main lifts (squat, trap bar deadlift, bench press), pick a starting weight you could lift for 8 reps with good form, then use it for sets of 5. That is your week 1 weight.

Progression scheme: linear. Add 5 lbs to upper body lifts and 10 lbs to lower body lifts each week if all sets were completed at the prescribed RPE. If a lift felt heavier than RPE 8, repeat the same weight next week.

### Week 4 Deload

Reduce all working weights to 70 percent of week 3 loads. Reduce sets on main lifts from 4 to 2. Keep accessories at normal volume but with weights you could lift comfortably for 12 reps. The point is active recovery and movement practice, not a missed week.

## Phase 2: Build (Weeks 5-8)

Volume rises here. Add a fourth set to one accessory lift per session. Rep ranges on accessories drift slightly higher (8-10 becomes 10-12 on the second exercise of each session). Main lifts stay at 4 x 5 with continued linear progression.

Switch to double progression on accessories: pick a rep range (for example 8-12). Start at the bottom of the range. When you can hit the top of the range with good form on all sets, add weight next session and drop back to the bottom. Repeat.

Walking volume should be at minimum 8,000 steps daily by week 6. This is non-negotiable for fat loss phases and strongly recommended even at maintenance.

### Week 8 Deload

Same protocol as week 4. Two-week-out warning sign: if you are dreading sessions, sleeping poorly, or stalling on weights you previously moved, take the deload one week early. There is no medal for grinding through.

## Phase 3: Intensify (Weeks 9-12)

Volume drops and intensity rises. Main lifts shift from 4 x 5 to 5 x 3 at RPE 8-9. Accessory volume drops by one set per exercise. You should feel fresher between sessions despite the heavier loads, because total work has decreased.

This is the phase where weight-room PRs typically appear. Keep form strict. A missed rep at RPE 9 with clean form is a successful set. A grinder with rounded back is a missed set regardless of whether the weight moved.

### Week 12 Deload and Reset

Week 12 is a true deload, then a transition. Reduce volume to 60 percent. After week 12, you have two paths:

1. Run the program a second time, starting Phase 1 with the heavier weights you ended Phase 3 on as your new ceiling.
2. Switch to a different program for a 4-8 week mental and physical break, then return.

Both work. Pick whichever sustains your consistency.

## Progression Rules Summary

- Main lifts: linear progression in Phase 1, continued linear in Phase 2, doubles or triples in Phase 3.
- Accessory lifts: double progression throughout.
- If you miss reps two sessions in a row on a main lift, drop the weight by 10 percent and rebuild.
- Never skip the deload. The deload is what allows the next phase to push.

## Substitutions

If a movement does not work for you, substitute intelligently:

- Back squat aggravates lower back: use safety bar squat or front squat
- Conventional deadlift aggravates lower back: use trap bar deadlift or rack pull from mid-shin
- Barbell bench aggravates shoulder: use neutral-grip dumbbell press or floor press
- Overhead barbell press aggravates shoulder: use landmine press or seated dumbbell press
- Pull-ups too hard: use band-assisted pull-ups or lat pulldowns

Substitutions are tools, not excuses. If you are substituting because something is uncomfortable on day one, do the substitution. If you are substituting because you do not feel like squatting today, do the squat.
