# Chapter 8: Supplement Decision Tree

The supplement industry sells hope in capsules. Most of what you can buy at a supplement store does nothing measurable, and a meaningful fraction does less than nothing once you account for cost and the false sense that the pill is doing the work the training and food should be doing.

A handful of supplements are worth taking. The rest are not. This chapter sorts them into three tiers so you can stop wondering and start training.

The opening principle, said plainly: most supplements are optional. Training and nutrition are not. If your training is inconsistent and your protein intake is below 0.8 g per pound of bodyweight, no supplement on this list will change your body composition. Get the foundation right first.

This chapter is general guidance, not medical advice. Bloodwork beats opinion. If you have any health condition, take any prescription medication, or are unsure whether a supplement is appropriate for you, talk to your physician before starting it.

## Tier 1: Always Consider

These three have the strongest evidence base, the lowest cost, the cleanest safety profile, and the broadest applicability. Most men over 35 should consider all three.

### Creatine Monohydrate, 5 g per day

The most-studied performance supplement in existence. Increases muscle phosphocreatine stores, which improves repeat-effort performance (the second through fifth heavy set of a lift, for example). Modest but real effects on strength, lean mass, and recovery. Some evidence for cognitive benefits, particularly under sleep deprivation.

Take 5 grams per day, every day, with any meal. Timing does not matter. No loading phase is necessary; the difference between loading and a steady 5 g daily is two to three weeks of slightly faster saturation. Buy plain creatine monohydrate. Brand premiums are not justified by the evidence.

Cost: roughly $0.10-0.20 per serving from any reputable brand.

### Vitamin D3, 2,000-5,000 IU per day

Vitamin D is technically a hormone. Deficiency is widespread, particularly in men who work indoors, live above 35 degrees latitude, or have darker skin. Optimal serum levels are generally accepted to be in the 30-50 ng/mL range, with many performance and longevity researchers arguing for the upper end.

Dosing depends on baseline status, which is why bloodwork is useful (a 25-hydroxyvitamin D test is cheap and commonly available). Without bloodwork, 2,000 IU daily is a reasonable starting dose for most men. If you live in a northern climate, work indoors, or rarely see direct sunlight, 4,000-5,000 IU is more appropriate during winter months.

Take with a meal containing fat for better absorption.

### Omega-3 EPA + DHA, 2-3 g combined per day

Omega-3 fatty acids support cardiovascular health, may modestly reduce exercise-induced inflammation, and likely support brain and joint health. The relevant dose is the EPA + DHA content, not the total fish oil content. A 1,000 mg fish oil capsule may contain only 300 mg of EPA + DHA. Read the label.

Target 2-3 grams of combined EPA + DHA per day, ideally from fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) two to three times per week, supplemented with capsules or liquid fish oil to fill the gap. If fatty fish is not in your diet, supplement the full amount.

Cost varies. Quality matters here; cheap fish oil oxidizes and tastes like a marine accident. Reputable brands cost $20-40 per month for a meaningful dose.

## Tier 2: Situational

These have decent evidence in specific situations. Take them when the situation applies. Skip them otherwise.

### Whey Protein Powder

Not magic. It is simply protein. The reason it is on this list is convenience: hitting 180-220 grams of protein per day from whole food is challenging for many men, particularly with a busy work schedule. One scoop of whey provides 20-25 grams of protein for around $1, and it takes 30 seconds to mix.

Buy any reputable brand. Whey isolate digests slightly faster and contains less lactose than concentrate, useful for sensitive stomachs. Otherwise the differences are minimal.

If you can hit your protein target from food consistently, you do not need protein powder. If you cannot, get the powder.

### Magnesium Glycinate, 200-400 mg in the evening

Magnesium is involved in muscle relaxation, sleep regulation, and hundreds of enzymatic processes. Many men are mildly deficient, particularly those who sweat heavily or eat processed-food-heavy diets. Magnesium glycinate is the form best tolerated for sleep purposes; magnesium citrate works as well but has a more pronounced laxative effect at higher doses.

Take 200-400 mg in the evening, 30-60 minutes before bed. The effect on sleep is modest but real for most people. Skip if your diet is rich in leafy greens, nuts, seeds, and whole grains and you sleep well.

### Caffeine, 100-200 mg pre-workout

Caffeine is the most reliable pre-workout supplement that exists. Improves perceived effort, peak power output, and endurance. Most pre-workout formulas are essentially caffeine plus expensive marketing.

100-200 mg taken 30-45 minutes before training is sufficient for most men. A cup of strong coffee provides 95-150 mg. Tolerance builds over time; cycling off for 1-2 weeks every few months restores sensitivity. Avoid caffeine within 8-10 hours of bed (see Chapter 6).

If you train in the evening, skip it. The sleep cost outweighs the training benefit.

## Tier 3: Skip Unless Bloodwork Justifies

Almost everything else. The list of supplements with marketing budgets far exceeding their evidence base is long. Below are the most common ones men over 35 ask about.

| Supplement | Verdict |
|------------|---------|
| Testosterone boosters (tribulus, fenugreek, etc.) | Skip. Effects on serum testosterone are negligible in men with normal levels. |
| BCAAs | Skip if hitting protein target. Whole protein contains BCAAs. |
| Glutamine | Skip. Generally accepted as ineffective for muscle gain in healthy adults. |
| Pre-workout blends | Skip the proprietary blends. Use plain caffeine. |
| Multivitamin | Optional. Worthwhile if your diet is narrow; otherwise food covers it. |
| ZMA | Skip. Magnesium alone is sufficient. |
| Ashwagandha | Mixed evidence for stress/sleep. Reasonable to try; not first-line. |
| Beta-alanine | Marginal for high-rep lifting. Causes harmless skin tingling. Optional. |
| Citrulline malate | Marginal. Some pump benefit. Optional. |
| Mass gainer powders | Skip. They are sugar with whey and a markup. Eat real food. |
| Fat burners | Skip. They are caffeine with extras. Use plain caffeine if needed. |
| HMB | Skip for most. Possibly useful in detrained or older populations. |
| Greens powders | Optional. Cheaper to eat vegetables. |

## Bloodwork: The Foundation Most Men Skip

If you can afford one thing in your supplement budget, spend it on a comprehensive blood panel before you spend a dollar on capsules. A reasonable annual panel for men over 35 includes:

- Complete metabolic panel
- Lipid panel
- Total and free testosterone, SHBG
- Fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HbA1c
- 25-hydroxyvitamin D
- Ferritin
- TSH (thyroid)
- Comprehensive blood count

Costs range from $100 to $300 depending on country and provider. The information is worth multiples of any supplement stack.

## The Final Word

If you take only Tier 1 (creatine, vitamin D, omega-3) and nothing else, you have captured 90 percent of the realistic supplement value available. Spend the money you would have spent on Tier 3 on better food, better sleep, or a session with a good coach.

The pills do not build the body. The work does.
