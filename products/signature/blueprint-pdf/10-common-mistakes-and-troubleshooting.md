# Chapter 10: Common Mistakes and Troubleshooting

Every man over 35 who picks up a barbell again makes at least three of these mistakes in the first month. The difference between the men who succeed and those who quit is not genetics or willpower: it is recognizing the mistake early and correcting course instead of spiraling.

## Mistake 1: Program Hopping

You run a program for two weeks, do not see visible abs, and switch to another program you found on social media. Repeat every 14 days for six months. Net result: zero progress and a growing belief that nothing works for you.

The fix: commit to one program for 12 full weeks. No program produces visible results in two weeks. Adaptations happen in 4-to-6-week blocks. If you are following the program in Chapter 4, trust the phases. Evaluate at week 12, not week 2.

## Mistake 2: Ego Lifting Past Form Breakdown

At 25, you could get away with ugly reps. At 40, a single rep where your lower back rounds under load can cost you six weeks of recovery. The risk-to-reward ratio has changed permanently.

The fix: use RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) as your governor. If your form breaks before you hit the target RPE, the weight is too heavy. Lower it. Nobody at the gym is tracking your numbers, and the ones who are also do not care.

## Mistake 3: Cutting Calories Too Aggressively

A 1,000-calorie deficit at 38 does not produce twice the fat loss of a 500-calorie deficit. It produces muscle loss, tanked testosterone, irritability, and a weekend binge that erases the deficit entirely.

The fix: aim for a 300-to-500-calorie daily deficit if fat loss is the goal. At this rate, expect 0.5 to 1 pound per week. Slower is sustainable. Sustainable is what actually works over 12 weeks.

## Mistake 4: Skipping Deloads

Deload weeks feel like wasted time. They are not. After 3 to 4 weeks of progressive overload, connective tissue, joints, and your central nervous system need a recovery period. Tendons adapt slower than muscles. Pushing through accumulates fatigue that eventually surfaces as a nagging shoulder or a knee that clicks.

The fix: take the deload at weeks 4, 8, and 12 as prescribed in the program. Use 50 to 60 percent of your working weights. Focus on movement quality. You will come back stronger in week 5 than if you had powered through.

## Mistake 5: All-or-Nothing Thinking

You miss Monday's session, so you skip the whole week. You eat pizza on Thursday, so you abandon the meal plan for the weekend. One imperfect day becomes a lost month.

The fix: imperfect consistency beats perfect inconsistency every time. If you train 2 days out of 3 this week, that is 67 percent adherence, and 67 percent adherence over 12 weeks produces meaningful results. Zero percent adherence produces nothing.

## Mistake 6: Undereating Protein

Most men over 35 eat roughly half the protein they need for muscle retention, let alone growth. If your lunch is a sandwich and your dinner is pasta with marinara, you are probably hitting 60 to 80 grams. You need 150 or more.

The fix: 1 gram per pound of bodyweight, every day. Front-load it: 40 grams at breakfast, 40 at lunch, 40 at dinner, and fill the gap with a shake or a high-protein snack. This single change produces more visible results than any supplement.

## Mistake 7: Drinking Away Your Training

Alcohol suppresses muscle protein synthesis for up to 24 hours, disrupts deep sleep cycles (where growth hormone peaks), and adds 150 to 300 empty calories per drink. Two beers on Friday and Saturday nights quietly undo a meaningful portion of the week's training stimulus.

The fix: this is not an abstinence lecture. If you drink, limit it to 1 to 2 drinks, 1 to 2 nights per week, and never on training days. Keep the drinks away from the 24-hour window after a hard session.

## Mistake 8: Ignoring Sleep

You can train perfectly and eat perfectly, but if you sleep 5 hours a night, your cortisol stays elevated, your testosterone stays suppressed, and your recovery runs at half speed. Sleep is the single most underrated variable.

The fix: 7 to 9 hours in a cool, dark room. No screens 30 minutes before bed. Consistent wake time (even weekends). This is covered in depth in Chapter 6.

## Mistake 9: No Tracking

If you do not log your lifts, you cannot prove you are progressing. If you do not weigh yourself, you cannot know whether your nutrition strategy is working. Memory is unreliable. "I think I lifted more last week" is not data.

The fix: a notebook or an app, updated after every session. Record exercise, sets, reps, weight, RPE. Review weekly. This takes 2 minutes per session and is the difference between guessing and knowing.

## Mistake 10: No Walking

Walking does not feel like exercise. That is exactly why it works. 7,000 to 10,000 steps per day increases your non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) by 200 to 400 calories, improves insulin sensitivity, aids recovery between sessions, and costs zero joint wear.

The fix: walk 20 to 30 minutes after meals. Park farther away. Take phone calls standing. Track steps with any cheap wearable. The goal is movement volume, not intensity.

## When to Seek Help

If you have persistent joint pain that does not improve with deloads and mobility work, see a physiotherapist. If you have symptoms of low testosterone (chronic fatigue, low libido, depression, inability to recover), get bloodwork through your doctor. If you have a history of cardiac events, get medical clearance before starting any resistance training program.

This book gives you the framework. A qualified professional gives you the diagnosis. Do not use one as a substitute for the other.
