# Chapter 5: Nutrition, Macros, and Meal Structure

The training program in Chapter 4 supplies the stimulus. Nutrition supplies the raw material. You can train perfectly for twelve weeks and see almost no body composition change if your nutrition is wrong, and you can eat reasonably well with no training and lose weight without building a single pound of muscle. The two work together. This chapter teaches you how to set up the nutrition side.

## The Three Numbers That Matter

Macros are protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Each gram of protein and carbohydrate contains about 4 calories. Each gram of fat contains about 9 calories. Alcohol contains 7 calories per gram, which we will address separately later.

Your daily intake target has three components:

1. Total calorie target (set by your goal)
2. Protein target (set by your bodyweight)
3. Fat and carbohydrate split (set by preference within constraints)

Get these three numbers right and the meal-by-meal details largely take care of themselves.

## Step 1: Calculate Your TDEE

TDEE means Total Daily Energy Expenditure, the total calories your body burns in a day. We use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is the most accurate of the common formulas for adults.

For men: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age in years) + 5

To convert: 1 lb = 0.453 kg, 1 inch = 2.54 cm.

Then multiply BMR by your activity factor:

| Activity Level | Multiplier |
|----------------|------------|
| Sedentary (desk job, no exercise) | 1.2 |
| Lightly active (desk job, 1-3 light workouts/week) | 1.375 |
| Moderately active (3-5 workouts/week, this program) | 1.55 |
| Very active (physical job, 5+ workouts/week) | 1.725 |
| Extremely active (manual labor, twice-daily training) | 1.9 |

Most readers of this book fall in the 1.375 to 1.55 range. If your job is a desk and you train three days a week, use 1.4. If you also walk 8,000+ steps daily, use 1.5. Round numbers are fine; this is an estimate, not a measurement.

A worked example appears in Chapter 11.

## Step 2: Set Your Goal Calories

| Goal | Calorie Target |
|------|----------------|
| Fat loss | TDEE minus 400-500 calories |
| Maintenance | TDEE |
| Lean gain | TDEE plus 200-300 calories |

For most readers, the right goal for the first 12 weeks is fat loss if you are above 18 percent body fat, or lean gain if you are below 12 percent. In between is judgment. When in doubt, run a 12-week fat loss phase first, see your structure when leaner, then decide.

A 400-500 calorie deficit produces about 0.7 to 1.0 pounds of fat loss per week for most men. Faster loss is possible but typically costs muscle and produces poor adherence.

## Step 3: Set Your Protein Target

Protein target: 1 gram per pound of bodyweight per day, minimum.

A 180 lb man eats 180 grams of protein per day. A 220 lb man eats 220 grams. This is the floor, not the ceiling. For men over 35 in a fat loss phase, going as high as 1.2 grams per pound is reasonable and helps preserve muscle. For lean gain, 1.0 grams per pound is sufficient.

Why this matters: protein has the highest thermic effect of any macro (you burn 20-30 percent of its calories digesting it), the highest satiety per calorie, and is the only macro that directly builds muscle. Under-eating protein is the single most common nutrition mistake in this demographic.

## Step 4: Set Fat and Carbohydrate

Fat: 0.35 to 0.50 grams per pound of bodyweight per day. Below this range, hormone production suffers. Above this range, you simply have fewer carbohydrates to fuel training. A 180 lb man should eat 65-90 grams of fat per day.

Carbohydrate: fills the remaining calorie budget.

Worked example for a 180 lb man at 2,400 calories:

- Protein: 180 g x 4 = 720 calories
- Fat: 75 g x 9 = 675 calories
- Remaining: 2,400 - 720 - 675 = 1,005 calories from carbs
- Carbs: 1,005 / 4 = 251 g

## Meal Structure and Timing

Meal frequency does not matter for fat loss or muscle gain. Three meals per day works. Five meals per day works. Two meals per day works for most men, though it can make hitting protein harder. Pick the schedule that fits your life and let it be.

What does matter is peri-workout nutrition. The meal before training and the meal after training should each contain at least 30-50 grams of protein and a meaningful amount of carbohydrate. This is the only timing rule that consistently shows benefit in the research for trained lifters. Beyond that, eat when you are hungry and at the times that fit your day.

## Food Categories (Not Menus)

Build meals from these categories. The book deliberately does not give you a 30-day menu, because compliance with someone else's menu is worse than compliance with foods you actually like.

Protein sources (aim for 30-50 g per meal):
chicken breast, lean beef, eggs and egg whites, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, white fish (cod, tilapia), salmon, tuna, lean pork, whey or casein protein powder, tempeh, edamame.

Carbohydrate sources (focus on minimally processed):
rice (white and brown), oats, potatoes (white and sweet), pasta, bread (sourdough or whole grain), fruit (berries, bananas, apples), beans and lentils, quinoa.

Fat sources:
olive oil, avocado, nuts and nut butters, fatty fish, whole eggs, full-fat Greek yogurt, dark chocolate, cheese in moderation.

Vegetables and high-volume foods (eat freely):
leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, peppers, cucumbers, tomatoes, mushrooms, zucchini, asparagus, berries.

## Alcohol: Harm Reduction

Alcohol does not have to ruin your results, but it is the single biggest variable lever most men in this demographic could pull. Three honest facts:

1. Alcohol is 7 calories per gram and provides no useful nutrients. A typical beer is 150 calories. A typical glass of wine is 120 calories. Three drinks is 400-450 calories that come out of your daily budget.
2. Alcohol consumed within 4 hours of sleep significantly degrades sleep quality, particularly REM and deep sleep, which directly impacts recovery from training.
3. Heavy drinking sessions (4+ drinks) acutely suppress muscle protein synthesis and testosterone for 24-48 hours afterwards.

The harm reduction position: occasional moderate drinking (1-2 drinks, not within 3 hours of sleep, not on training days) is compatible with this program. Frequent or heavy drinking is not. If your weekly total is more than 7 drinks, this is your highest-leverage change.

## Tracking, Then Not Tracking

Track everything you eat for the first 4-6 weeks using a tool like MacroFactor, Cronometer, or MyFitnessPal. This is not forever. The point is to learn what your meals actually contain, so that after the tracking period you can eat by sight and stay close to your targets.

After 6 weeks, most men can eyeball their intake within 200 calories per day. Re-track for two weeks any time progress stalls or after a holiday season.
