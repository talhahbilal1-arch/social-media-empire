# Chapter 3: The Five Pillars

Every plan in this book rests on five pillars. Drop one and the structure leans. Drop two and it falls. The next chapters cover each in detail. This chapter is the overview so you can see the whole shape before you read the parts.

## Pillar 1: Training

Three resistance training sessions per week, built around compound movements scaled for joint health, with progressive overload tracked across twelve weeks. Training is the stimulus. Without it, nutrition becomes a diet, and diets without training cost you muscle along with fat. The program in Chapter 4 is the load-bearing wall of the entire system. If you have time for only one pillar in any given week, train.

## Pillar 2: Nutrition

Macro-based eating built around protein, with calorie totals matched to your goal (fat loss, maintenance, or lean gain) and carbohydrates concentrated around training. You will not count grams forever. You will count for long enough to learn what your meals actually contain, and then you will use that intuition for the rest of your life. Protein is the priority macro: roughly 1 gram per pound of bodyweight, every day, no exceptions. Chapter 5 has the full system.

## Pillar 3: Recovery

Sleep, stress management, and structured rest days. Seven to nine hours of sleep is non-negotiable for men over 35. Sleep is when muscle is built, hormones are restored, and the central nervous system clears the fatigue of training. No supplement, no training program, and no nutrition plan can compensate for chronic sleep debt. Chapter 6 covers sleep mechanics, stress tools, and how to know when you are recovering well enough to keep pushing.

## Pillar 4: Mobility

A daily 10-minute mobility routine covering hips, thoracic spine, shoulders, and ankles. This is the pillar most men skip and most men regret skipping. The cost of not doing it is gradual: tighter, then sore, then injured, then sidelined for six weeks. The cost of doing it is ten minutes a day. Chapter 7 walks through the exact movements.

## Pillar 5: Consistency

The pillar that holds up the other four. None of the work in this book is hard in the moment. The hard part is doing it again on the day you do not feel like it, and again the week after, and again three months from now. Consistency is built by reducing friction (program your training, prep your protein, lay out your gym clothes) and by accepting that imperfect execution sustained for twelve weeks beats perfect execution sustained for six days.

The plan does not need to be perfect. It needs to be repeated. That is the entire game.
